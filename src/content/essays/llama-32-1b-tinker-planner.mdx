The planner LLM is the part of Perseus that reads a query, looks at
evidence, and emits a JSON object naming the next tool to call. Every
node expansion in the MCTS loop is one planner call. The cost of that
call — quality, latency, dollars — multiplies through every retrieval
trajectory Perseus runs.

Replacing `gpt-5-nano` (the teacher) with a self-hosted small LM was
the explicit goal of the 2026-05-13 → 2026-05-17 planner training
arc. As of 2026-05-17 18:02 IST the production checkpoint is
`llama32-1b-epoch3-v3`: a 1.24B-parameter vanilla `LlamaForCausalLM`,
LoRA-finetuned via Tinker on 180,720 distilled `gpt-5-nano-medium`
trajectories, served on cato GPU 2/6 via vLLM at `:19320`.

This essay is the load-bearing argument for why **that particular
checkpoint, on that particular architecture, on that particular
hardware** is what's running production.

---

## 1. Why vanilla Llama at all

The shortlist from the [planner base bake-off](/essays/planner-base-bakeoff/)
had four candidates:

| Family | Params | Arch | V100 outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Falcon-H1-Tiny-Coder-90M | ~91M | FalconH1ForCausalLM (Mamba + attention hybrid) | **600 steps/hr** — Mamba state-update kernels have no fused sm_70 path. 64–110 h/epoch projected. Dropped. |
| Gemma3-270m | 268M | Gemma3ForCausalLM (15 sliding + 3 full attn) | 3,700 steps/hr. Trained to completion. Schema-leak failure in production. |
| LFM2-350M | ~354M | Lfm2ForCausalLM (conv + full hybrid) | SFT ran to completion. Never wired to a serving shim. Effectively shelved. |
| **meta-llama/Llama-3.2-1B** | **1.24B** | **LlamaForCausalLM (hidden=2048, 16 layers, GQA 32/8)** | **The pick.** |

The decision was *not* "biggest wins". The decision rested on four
multiplicative properties, all of which had to be non-zero
simultaneously.

### Q (quality): vanilla LlamaForCausalLM hits the Gemma ceiling head-on

Gemma3-270m trained to `val_loss ≈ 1.10` and emitted valid JSON
one-shot. Multi-turn it leaked literal schema placeholders — `"..."`
and `{...}` strings — into the planner loop. The parse + repair retry
chain in `planner/parse.rs` caught some of these; the production
fallback path was `mock://local` or back to `gpt-5-nano` over the
OpenAI API. Empirically, prompt-only intervention against gpt-5-nano
hit a ceiling at 53% `search_text` dominance no matter how the prompt
was framed. The structural fix lives in
`runtime::diversity_prior_multiplier`, but the **architectural** fix
is more parameters in the right place.

Llama-3.2-1B at rank-64 LoRA hits `val_loss = 1.098` on the same
180k corpus where Gemma3-270m hit ~1.10. The Llama checkpoint also
*doesn't leak schema placeholders* — multi-turn JSON stays clean
through the entire MCTS tree. That property is what made it the
production candidate.

### J (json-mode): vLLM 0.9.x + XGrammar work on it

vLLM's `guided_json` (XGrammar backend) gives schema guarantees
without paying the pre-XGrammar 200-second Triton-`outlines` cold
start. Gemma3 + LoRA tripped vLLM's `TransformersModel does not
support LoRA` codepath; the team fell back to a custom FastAPI shim
around `peft + transformers` on `:19200`. That path is unreliable
under multi-turn load.

Vanilla `LlamaForCausalLM` is a first-class vLLM model class. The
`merge_and_unload()` → `vllm serve` path is exactly the path vLLM is
tuned for. JSON-mode works. Tool-calling works (left disabled by
default per `PERSEUS_LLM_TREE_GUIDED_JSON=1`). No fork.

### T (throughput): the Triton prefix-prefill fork saves the architecture

V100 is `sm_70`. vLLM's V1 engine refuses to start on sm_70 — we pin
to V0 with `--enforce-eager`. The CUDA-graph capture is silently
disabled on Volta; the flag stops vLLM from even trying.

vLLM's standard `prefix_prefill.py` Triton kernel uses
`tl.dot(..., input_precision="ieee")` and
`tl.dot(..., out_dtype=tl.float32)`. On sm_70 the Triton IR cannot
lower an f32-output mma — kernel compilation either fails or silently
falls back to a slow reference path.

The fork at `services/perseus_planner_server/prefix_prefill_v100.py`
forces fp16 mma (`HMMA.884.F32.F16.F16`):
- No `input_precision=` kwarg on `tl.dot`.
- Q/K/V explicitly cast to fp16 before `tl.dot`.
- `out_dtype=tl.float16` followed by manual `.to(tl.float32)` and add
  to f32 accumulator.
- `BLOCK_M=BLOCK_N=64` (smaller tiles fit sm_70 shared memory).
- `num_warps=4, num_stages=2` (no async copy on Volta).
- Drops fp8 / sliding-window / skip-decode branches.

Validated standalone: **245.7 ms full prefill (q=3008, ctx=0) →
9.73 ms cached prefix (3000 ctx + 50 delta) = 25.2× speedup.** Target
was 10×.

This kernel is **not yet wired into the live engine**. The live
serving path (`:19320`) uses vLLM's stock PagedAttention with its
page-granularity prefix cache. The fork lives in
`services/perseus_planner_server/` as a validated standalone
function plus a placeholder hook at the engine layer; the wiring is a
~250-LOC layer-by-layer projection unroll. But the existence of a
proven sm_70-friendly prefix-prefill kernel is what made the Llama
path viable — it's the insurance policy that says "if vLLM's stock
kernel regresses on Volta, we have a paved path back to tok/s parity."

### F (fits): 1B fp16 = 2.4 GB, V100 has 32 GB

A 1.24B-parameter Llama in fp16 takes ~2.4 GB of weights. The merged
checkpoint on disk is exactly that:
`tinker_merged/llama32-1b-epoch3-v3/` = 2.4 GB single
`model.safetensors`. A V100-SXM3-32GB has ~28 GB usable VRAM after
PagedAttention bookkeeping and the KV cache, leaving ~25 GB
exclusively for the KV cache itself.

That budget is what makes `--max-num-seqs 48 --max-num-batched-tokens
24576 --enforce-eager` feasible on a single V100. 48 concurrent
sequences with 24,576 tokens of paged KV is well within budget.
Tensor parallel across 4–8 V100s was tried; the answer was the same
single-stream tok/s. 1B fits cleanly on a single V100; TP buys
nothing.

`Q × J × T × F > 0`. The product is what matters. Falcon failed T
(throughput); Gemma failed Q (multi-turn quality); LFM2 failed at
the integration step (no serving shim). Llama is the only candidate
where every multiplicative term is strictly positive.

## 2. The LoRA math, briefly

LoRA decomposes the weight update as a low-rank product:

$$W' = W + \frac{\alpha}{r} B A$$

where $W \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is the frozen base weight,
$B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d}$
are the trainable adapter matrices, and $r \ll d$ is the rank. Only
$BA$ moves; the base $W$ stays frozen.

For Llama-3.2-1B with `hidden_size=2048` and rank `r=64`, each
target projection (`q_proj`, `k_proj`, `v_proj`, `o_proj`) trains
$2 \cdot 2048 \cdot 64 = 262,144$ params instead of the full
$2048^2 = 4.2,M$ — a **16× reduction in trainable parameters per
projection**. Across 16 decoder layers × 4 projections × 16× saving,
the rank-64 adapter is ~33M trainable params vs the full 1.24B base.

The exported adapter is 213 MB. The merged FP16 checkpoint is 2.4 GB
— the same as the base model, because `merge_and_unload()` folds the
LoRA delta back into $W$ in place. After merge, the model is a vanilla
Llama-3.2-1B with a perturbed weight matrix; vLLM doesn't know it was
ever a LoRA adapter.

The Tinker rank cap for Llama-3.2-1B is **128**. Higher ranks (the
`v3_r256_short` launch attempt) get rejected at submit time with
`tinker.BadRequestError: 400 — lora_config.rank 256 exceeds max LoRA
rank 128 for model meta-llama/Llama-3.2-1B`. The sweep accepted that
ceiling and stayed at rank ∈ {64, 128}.

## 3. Tinker, in one diagram

Tinker is a remote-SDK LoRA fine-tune service. The client
(orchestrator script on Sam's laptop) calls `forward_backward()` and
`optim_step()` against a remote optimizer state. The training script
itself is local; the model lives on Tinker's GPUs.

```
local laptop                          Tinker remote (H100 fleet)
─────────────                         ──────────────────────────
train_ultra.py                        base = meta-llama/Llama-3.2-1B
  build Datum                            (frozen weights)
  send forward_backward(batch)   ───→   forward + backward
                                         emit gradients into BA
  send optim_step(adam_state)    ───→   apply AdamW step to B, A
  poll loss                      ←───   {step_loss, val_loss}
                                ...
  on final:
  uri = "tinker://<run-id>:train:0/weights/shard00_final"
  export.py uri → PEFT tar       ←───   signed download URL
  unpack to local HF dir                  adapter_config.json
                                          adapter_model.safetensors
```

The pipeline-depth trick in `train_ultra.py` keeps **12** concurrent
`forward_backward` + `optim_step` futures in flight. That single knob
took the trainer from ~600 steps/hr to ~3,700 steps/hr on the same
remote — the network round-trip is the bottleneck, not the GPU. The
asyncio scheduler is the unsung star of the [Tinker 3-epoch
ultra](/essays/tinker-3-epoch-ultra/) run.

Two more `train_ultra.py` features matter for production quality:

1. **Curation**: `--min-file-recall 0.5 --min-terminal-reward -1.0`
   filters rows before packing. 180,720 → 178,848 (keeps 98.96% — the
   filter is permissive enough to be near-no-op at the production
   thresholds but the knob exists for [v3_superclean's strict
   variant](/essays/tinker-lora-bakeoff/) at fr≥0.8 / tr≥0.5 which
   cut to 88,918 rows).
2. **First-fit bin-packing**: multiple short examples packed into one
   `max_seq_len=4096` window. The corpus has heterogeneous lengths
   (some planner steps are 200 tokens, some are 3,800). Bin-packing
   bumps utilization from ~45% to ~92% per sequence, which is most
   of the throughput win.

The production hyperparameters were: rank=64, α=128, target
modules `q_proj,k_proj,v_proj,o_proj`, base_lr=2e-4, batch=16,
max_seq_len=4096, pipeline=12, 3 epochs over 178,848 rows = 10,892
steps. Best `val_loss = 1.098 @ step 10,500` — exported at that
step, not at `shard00_final` (10,892).

## 4. The export pipeline

```
1. Tinker URI:
   tinker://0951cfea-85a6-5a21-910e-fa0b646b9d5d:train:0/weights/shard00_step010500
              └─────────────────┬───────────────┘
                          run-id

2. tinker_train/export.py:
   - POST resolve(uri) → signed download URL
   - GET tar.gz → /tmp/peft_export.tar.gz
   - extract to: tinker_exports/llama32-1b-epoch3-v3/step_10500/
     contents: adapter_config.json (r=64, α=128, q/k/v/o)
               adapter_model.safetensors (213 MB)
               tokenizer.json / tokenizer_config.json (passthrough)

3. peft.merge_and_unload():
   model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(base_model)
   model = PeftModel.from_pretrained(model, adapter_dir)
   merged = model.merge_and_unload()
   merged.save_pretrained(merged_dir, safe_serialization=True)

   output: tinker_merged/llama32-1b-epoch3-v3/  (2.4 GB)
           ├── config.json          (vanilla Llama-3.2-1B config)
           ├── generation_config.json
           ├── model.safetensors    (single 2.4 GB file, fp16)
           ├── tokenizer.json
           └── tokenizer_config.json

4. vllm serve (since 2026-05-17 18:02 IST):
   vllm serve /home/cato-user/tinker_merged/llama32-1b-epoch3-v3 \
     --served-model-name llama32_1b_epoch3 \
     --host 0.0.0.0 --port 19320 \
     --max-num-seqs 48 \
     --max-num-batched-tokens 24576 \
     --enforce-eager \
     --dtype float16
```

Three flags on the vLLM command line are load-bearing:

- `--max-num-seqs 48` — the sweep launches up to 48 concurrent
  multi-bench workers, each issuing planner calls. PagedAttention
  needs the upper bound to allocate block tables.
- `--max-num-batched-tokens 24576` — sum of all in-flight sequences'
  decode-step tokens. 48 × 512 average context = 24,576.
- `--enforce-eager` — disables `torch.compile` / CUDA graph capture.
  Volta sm_70 doesn't support CUDA graph capture in vLLM V0;
  `--enforce-eager` is the polite way of telling vLLM to not even
  try. Without it the engine crashes at startup.

The merged checkpoint is fp16 native. Why not GPTQ-INT4? Tried —
`decode_accel/quantized/llama32_1b_tinker_gptq_int4_g128` (1.5 GB,
auto-gptq 0.7.1, `damp_percent=0.01, desc_act=false, group_size=128`,
32 calibration examples). Result: **~15% wall-clock loss vs FP16
single-stream**. GPTQ Marlin kernels need sm_80+; V100 is sm_70 →
falls back to slower paths. The whole point of INT4 (Marlin
acceleration) doesn't exist on this hardware. FP16 native beats it.
The GPTQ copy is retained on `:19311` as an A/B fallback only.

## 6. The deployment gap with v3_bigseq

The Tinker bake-off shipped a quantitatively better variant than the
one running in production. **`v3_bigseq` hit val_loss = 0.932 at step
9,000.** The production checkpoint (`llama32-1b-epoch3-v3`) hit
val_loss = 1.098 at step 10,500. On the loss axis, that's a 15%
improvement — comparable to what you'd expect from a generation jump,
not a hyperparameter sweep.

The hypothesis behind `v3_bigseq` was: **seq-length matters more
than rank** on this corpus. The full 180k SFT rows have nontrivial
completion lengths from multi-tool planner outputs; rank-64 at
seq=8192 hit a strictly lower val than rank-128 at seq=4096. The
sweep validates that hypothesis cleanly.

`v3_bigseq` is exported (`tinker_exports/v3_bigseq/step_9000/`).
`v3_bigseq` is merged (`tinker_merged/v3_bigseq_step9000/`, 2.4 GB).
It is **not deployed**. No vLLM listener serves it.

Why not? Three reasons, in order of bluntness:

1. **No auto-promote pipeline.** The Tinker-eval rotation
   (`auto_eval_variants.py` + `benchmark_variants.py` at
   `cato:/home/cato-user/tinker_eval/`) watches for new LoRA
   checkpoints, exports them, merges them, launches per-variant
   `vllm serve` on GPU 2..6 / port 19301..19305 — but the leaderboard
   it builds is over a 5-case ripgrep suite where **only 1 of 5
   cases (ripgrep-1367) actually hits gold**. The signal is dominated
   by one case. Recall@5 with one positive doesn't separate variants
   well, so the leaderboard can't say "yes, swap production now."
2. **The benchmark suite to certify `v3_bigseq` doesn't exist yet.** A
   diversity-balanced 100-instance pool was built at
   `artifacts/autoresearch-instance-pool-v2.json`. Wiring it into the
   Tinker-eval rotation is a half-day task; it just hasn't happened.
3. **Val loss isn't quality.** A 0.932 vs 1.098 cross-entropy gap on
   teacher trajectories is plausibly an artifact of `v3_bigseq`
   seeing longer prefixes (it has more context to predict the next
   token from). Downstream lift on retrieval-quality metrics — the
   thing Perseus actually optimizes — is unmeasured.

This gap is the load-bearing piece of the [Tinker LoRA
bake-off](/essays/tinker-lora-bakeoff/) post-mortem. The team
produced a measurably better artifact and shipped the worse one.
That's not a training failure; that's a graduation-pipeline failure.

## 7. Why this wins, in one sentence

V100 kernel work pays for the bigger model. Specifically: the Triton
prefix-prefill fork at `services/perseus_planner_server/prefix_prefill_v100.py`
proves Volta can serve a 1B-parameter model at ~25× cached-prefix
speedup over the stock vLLM kernel. That validated kernel — plus the
fact that vanilla `LlamaForCausalLM` is a first-class vLLM model
class on V100 V0 with `--enforce-eager` — is what lets Llama-3.2-1B
win across throughput-bound concurrent workloads where Gemma3-270m
would be cheaper-per-call but unreliable, and where Falcon-H1-90M
would be cheapest-per-call but 6× slower in steps/hr at training time.

The single-line summary: **quality lift from Llama-1B over
Gemma-270m at long-context dominates the throughput cost on V100.**
That's the bet. As of 2026-05-17 18:02 IST, the bet is in production.

## 9. The honest disclaimer

`engram` production currently points `PERSEUS_LLM_TREE_ENDPOINTS` at
`https://api.openai.com/v1` with
`PERSEUS_LLM_TREE_MODEL=gpt-5-nano-2025-08-07`. The cato-hosted
Llamas are deployed, healthy, and probeable — but they are not on the
production traffic path. The pivot back to OpenAI direct was made
when the cato pool failed health-checks during the 2026-05-12 sweep.

So "the deployed planner" is true in the narrow sense — `:19320` has
been serving valid completions since 2026-05-17 18:02 — and not yet
true in the sweep-traffic sense. The pieces that gate moving sweep
traffic to cato are the same pieces that gate `v3_bigseq` deployment:
a downstream-quality benchmark on the 100-instance pool, a green
streak across the 120-minute stabilization window, and a working
gate-streak counter in `artifacts/eval-gate-state.json`.

When that gate flips, `:19320` becomes the production planner in
both senses. Until then, this essay documents the artifact, not the
traffic.
