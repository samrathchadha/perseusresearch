## The shape of the experiment

By the afternoon of 2026-05-16 the Llama-3.2-1B planner distillation pipeline
had a working trainer (`train_ultra.py`), a curated corpus (180,720 rows of
gpt-5-nano teacher trajectories at `cato:/home/cato-user/distill_corpus/plan.jsonl`),
and exactly one shipped checkpoint behind the production planner endpoint.
That checkpoint — the boring reference point `llama32-1b-epoch3-v3` — had
landed at val_loss 1.098 with the default recipe: rank=64 LoRA, base_lr=2e-4,
batch=16, seq=4096, 178,848 rows kept after curation, ~10,892 steps. It was
good enough to deploy and was being served at port 19320 of the cato pool.

The question on the table was not "can we beat the baseline?" — it was
"*which axis* moves the loss most when we push it?" The recipe has four
levers worth pulling independently:

1. **LoRA rank** — capacity in the adapter. r=64 is the `train_ultra.py`
   default; can more rank fit the corpus better?
2. **Sequence length** — `max_seq_len`. Longer windows mean each training
   example sees more of the planner's branching context, and the bin-packer
   wastes fewer tokens on padding between short examples.
3. **Data quality** — `min_file_recall` and `min_terminal_reward` thresholds
   in the curation filter. Tighter filters drop noisier rows. Less data,
   cleaner signal.
4. **Data scale** — `max_rows` cap on the corpus. Holding rank and seq
   constant, what does halving (or quartering) the corpus do?

The right way to answer these is to vary exactly one axis per variant.
A single launcher script (`launch_variants_2026-05-16.sh`) kicked off four
parallel Tinker SFT runs against the same base model
(`meta-llama/Llama-3.2-1B`) with `--shard-total 1` each, named for the axis
they probed:

- **`v3_r128`** — rank 64→128, everything else held at the baseline. The
  *capacity* probe.
- **`v3_bigseq`** — seq 4096→8192, rank held at 64. The *context* probe.
- **`v3_superclean`** — `fr ≥ 0.8, tr ≥ 0.5` (vs baseline `fr ≥ 0.5, tr ≥ -1.0`).
  88,918 of 180,720 rows survive. The *data-quality* probe.
- **`v3_r256_short`** — rank 64→256 with a smaller corpus
  (`max_rows=50000`). The high-capacity-small-data corner. The *rank-cap*
  probe, though that's not what we intended it to probe.

Three of the four trained to completion. The fourth never started.

## v3_r128: capacity at constant data

The capacity-baseline variant. Doubled the LoRA rank from 64 to 128, kept
everything else identical to the deployed baseline.

- Tinker run id: `c0743d5c-f93e-562d-a05e-9ae56935f94f`
- Config: lora_rank=128, base_lr=1e-4, batch_size=16, max_seq_len=4096,
  max_rows=200000, min_file_recall=0.5, min_terminal_reward=-1.0
- Rows kept: 178,848 of 180,720
- Steps: ~10,892
- Loss: train 1.601 → noisy 0.52–0.96 band by s10800; val 1.469 (s100) →
  **1.122 @ s10800**, plateau around step 7–8k
- Exported, merged, deployed at `:19305`

LoRA's parameter count under standard targeting (all attention projections
plus MLP gates) is:

$$
\Delta\theta = 2 \cdot r \cdot d \cdot L \cdot H
$$

where $r$ is the LoRA rank, $d$ is the hidden width, $L$ is the number of
transformer blocks, and $H$ counts the targeted projection matrices per
block (Q/K/V/O plus gate/up/down in Llama-3 ≈ 7, but the dominant
attention-only baseline is 4). For Llama-3.2-1B with $d=2048$, $L=22$,
$H=4$:

$$
r=64: \quad \Delta\theta = 2 \cdot 64 \cdot 2048 \cdot 22 \cdot 4 \approx 8.4\text{M params}
$$

$$
r=128: \quad \Delta\theta = 2 \cdot 128 \cdot 2048 \cdot 22 \cdot 4 \approx 16.8\text{M params}
$$

Twice the parameters. The val loss moved from 1.098 (baseline rank-64) to
1.122 — *worse* by 0.024 nats. The capacity probe came back negative.

There are at least three plausible readings:

1. The base model is small (1.2B params) and the SFT corpus is small (~700M
   tokens after curation, ~50M after bin-packing). r=64 is already past the
   knee — the extra capacity at r=128 isn't constrained by the data.
2. The LR schedule wasn't retuned for the larger adapter. r=128's effective
   step size in the LoRA subspace is different from r=64's at the same
   base_lr; we used 1e-4 vs the baseline's 2e-4 to be cautious, which may
   have under-fit the larger rank.
3. There's no real difference. 1.098 and 1.122 are well inside single-seed
   noise on a 180k-row corpus.

We didn't run the LR sweep to disambiguate. r128 was deployed at `:19305`
to serve as the rank-axis reference for downstream eval, not promoted.

## v3_bigseq: the variant that won

Doubled the context window from 4096 to 8192 tokens. Kept rank at 64,
batch at 16, base_lr at 2e-4 (the baseline value, not the cautious r128
1e-4). This was the variant nobody on the team predicted would win.

- Tinker run id: `62666feb-3950-5f05-950b-75ae3108b287`
- Config: lora_rank=64, base_lr=2e-4, batch_size=16, **max_seq_len=8192**,
  max_rows=200000
- Rows kept: 178,848 of 180,720
- Steps: ~9,386
- Loss: train 1.17 (s100) → ~0.79 band by s9300; val **1.272 → 0.932** —
  **lowest val loss of the sweep**, plateau by s8000
- Exported, merged, **not deployed**

0.932 vs the production baseline's 1.098 is a 0.166-nat drop in val loss
— a meaningful, well-outside-noise improvement. And it came from the one
axis nobody was excited about.

The mechanism is bin-packing fill rate. `train_ultra.py` first-fit packs
multiple short examples into each `max_seq_len` window before dispatching
the batch. The planner-trajectory corpus has a heavily right-skewed
length distribution: most rows are 1–3k tokens (a single planner step
with branch context and a tool call) but a long tail runs 5–10k+ (deeper
trees with accumulated evidence). At `max_seq_len=4096`:

- short rows (1–3k) pack 1–3 per window; fill rate ~60–75% before the
  pad token
- medium rows (3–4k) pack exactly 1; fill rate ~75–95%
- long rows (>4k) get **truncated** — the planner's late-context evidence
  is silently dropped before the loss ever sees it

At `max_seq_len=8192`:

- short rows pack 3–6 per window; fill rate ~80–90%
- medium rows pack 2; fill rate ~85–95%
- long rows fit whole; the long-tail of rich multi-step examples now
  contributes a full gradient instead of a truncated one

Two effects compound. First, the packed batch carries more *information per
gradient step* — the model sees more distinct planner trajectories in the
same wall-clock. Second, the long-tail examples — which are
disproportionately the planner steps where the model has to integrate
evidence across multiple tool calls, exactly the behavior we're trying
to distill — now train at all instead of being chopped.

bigseq's loss curve descends faster early (1.272 at s100 vs 1.469 for
r128 at the same step) because each step is denser; it plateaus lower
(0.932 vs 1.122) because the targets that survive training are richer.
The cost is wall-clock per step (longer attention) and GPU memory
(quadratic in seq length), neither of which mattered: it still finished
in the same Tinker SFT envelope as its siblings.

This is the variant that should have been deployed. It wasn't.

## v3_superclean: data quality at compute parity

The strictest curation filter actually run. Held rank at 64, base_lr at
2e-4, max_seq_len at 4096 — the baseline recipe. Pushed
`min_file_recall` from 0.5 to 0.8 and `min_terminal_reward` from -1.0 to
0.5.

- Tinker run id: `9bb4378c-163d-5f56-ad90-358595e375b5`
- Config: lora_rank=64, base_lr=2e-4, batch_size=16, max_seq_len=4096,
  max_rows=200000, **min_file_recall=0.8, min_terminal_reward=0.5**
- Rows kept: **88,918 of 180,720** (~half the baseline)
- Steps: ~5,416 (~half the baseline)
- Loss: train 1.24 → ~0.80 band; val 1.351 (s100) → **1.120 @ s5400**
- **Not exported, not merged, not deployed**

The headline: superclean matched r128 (1.120 vs 1.122) at *half* the
compute. The data-quality filter doubled the effective signal per row —
each row that passed the tighter threshold was, in expectation, a closer
imitation of a planner trajectory that actually succeeded at the
downstream task.

Half the rows × half the steps × matched val loss is the cleanest result
of the four variants. It says: at this corpus size, the bottleneck is
*signal density per row*, not raw row count. The 91,802 rows dropped by
the tighter filter were carrying noise that the model was wasting
capacity on.

So why wasn't superclean deployed? Because bigseq existed. By the time
the sweep finished — superclean done at 13:26, bigseq done at 23:00 the
same day — bigseq's 0.932 was on the table and superclean's 1.120 was
clearly dominated. The export pipeline never fired for superclean; the
merged dir was never produced; the deploy gate never opened.

This is the lesson the next two sections are about. Producing a better
result is not the same as shipping a better result.

## v3_r256_short: the variant the platform refused to run

The intended fourth corner of the sweep — high LoRA rank (256) on a small
corpus (50k rows) — was a deliberate test of the
high-capacity-small-data regime. The question: when data is the
bottleneck, does extra adapter capacity hurt (overfit) or help (memorize
the small set well)?

We never got an answer. At submission time Tinker returned:

```
tinker.BadRequestError: 400 — lora_config.rank 256 exceeds max LoRA rank
128 for model meta-llama/Llama-3.2-1B
```

The Tinker API caps LoRA rank at 128 for the Llama-3.2-1B base. The cap
is not documented prominently on the model card; we discovered it by
hitting it. The launcher logged the rejection, left an empty
`v3_r256_short/` dir on cato, and continued.

The mitigation was to re-launch as `v3_r128_short` — rank kept at the
maximum allowed (128), the other variables held the same:

- Tinker run id: `27cfe245-d439-5dfc-9c31-74663c0487c4`
- Config: lora_rank=128, base_lr=8e-5, batch_size=16, max_seq_len=4096,
  **max_rows=50000**, min_file_recall=0.5, min_terminal_reward=-1.0
- Rows kept: 50,000
- Steps: ~3,046
- Loss: train 1.41 (s100) → ~0.90 band; val 1.517 (s100) → **1.283 @ s3000** —
  worst val of the sweep
- Not exported

r128_short was the data-scaling reference point: same rank as r128, but
~28% of the rows. Val loss 1.283 vs r128's 1.122 = +0.161 nats *worse*
from cutting the corpus. The data axis matters; cut corpus 4× and lose
0.16 nats. (Compare bigseq, which gained 0.166 nats on the same baseline
purely by doubling seq length on the *same* corpus. Symmetrical
magnitude, opposite direction — the corpus contains signal we'd been
truncating, and cutting the corpus loses signal we'd been keeping.)

But the original question — "what does r=256 do?" — is still
unanswered. The platform's rank cap is a real constraint on the sweep
design space, not an inconvenience to route around. Designing a variant
the platform won't run produces a hole in the experimental matrix that
nothing else fills.

## The deployment gap

Of the four runs that finished, bigseq had the best val_loss by a wide
margin. r128 was deployed (mid-pack). superclean was the
compute-efficient result. bigseq, the *winner*, is still sitting on cato
at `cato:/home/cato-user/tinker_merged/v3_bigseq_step9000/` — 2.4 GB,
LoRA fused into the base, ready for `vllm serve` — with no listener.

The verdict line in the run catalog says, accurately:

> Deployed?: **no live vllm serve** — merged dir exists, no listener.
> Ready to deploy.

"Ready to deploy" is what we say when the technical artifact exists but
the social mechanism to ship it doesn't. The pipeline from "sweep
finishes" to "winner serves production traffic" had no automation. The
human step — read the metrics, pick the winner, run `deploy.sh`,
patch `PERSEUS_LLM_TREE_ENDPOINTS` — was waiting on a planner-recall
eval to confirm the val_loss improvement translated to downstream lift.

The eval didn't run. Other work intervened. The bigseq merge sat. The
production planner kept serving the boring reference point at `:19320`
(val_loss 1.098), and r128 sat alongside it at `:19305` (val_loss 1.122)
serving as the rank-axis comparison — neither of them the best variant
the sweep produced.

This is the same pattern that surfaces in every
"sweep-without-a-graduation-pipeline" story. The sweep optimizes the
metric. The pipeline ships the artifact. When the second doesn't exist,
the first turns into a shelf of winning checkpoints that nobody uses.

## Eight rules

These are written down here because the next sweep is already being
designed and the failure modes above are forgettable.

**1. State the gate before launching the sweep.**

"Whoever posts the lowest val_loss ships" is a gate. "If anyone beats
the baseline by 0.05 nats, ship them" is a gate. "Hold a meeting after
all four finish and decide" is *not* a gate — it's an intention, and
intentions don't survive the next week's interruptions. bigseq beat the
baseline by 0.166 nats. No gate fired because no gate was set.

**2. Sweep produces options; pipeline ships winners.**

These are two separate engineering artifacts. The first is a launcher
script + a metrics table. The second is an automated job that watches
the metrics table, applies the gate, and runs the deploy. We had the
first. We didn't have the second. The deploy step was a human action
behind an unwritten queue.

**3. Axes not hyperparams (each variant probes ONE axis).**

The four variants disagreed on exactly one input each (rank, seq, filter,
rows). That's why the result attribution is clean: bigseq's win is
attributable to seq length because nothing else about it differs from
the baseline. If we'd moved two axes per variant, the result table would
be a Latin square of confounders. One axis per variant is the discipline
that makes the post-hoc reading legible.

**4. The platform's rank cap is real (r256_short never ran).**

A rank-256 variant on Llama-3.2-1B isn't a variant; it's a 400 response.
Before designing a sweep, enumerate every variant against the platform's
declared capabilities — model card, API docs, the actual `submit`
endpoint's input validation. The hole left by `r256_short` is permanent
in this experimental matrix; we don't know what 256-rank capacity does
to this corpus, and won't unless the platform raises the cap or we
move to a different base model.

**5. Bin-packing fill rate matters for context-length comparisons.**

bigseq's win is not "longer context magically improves loss." It's
"longer context *given the corpus's length distribution* changes how
much of each row contributes to the gradient." A sweep that varies seq
length without measuring fill rate is implicitly varying *information
per step* without controlling for it. The right cross-tab for a
seq-length sweep is val_loss × fill_rate × truncation_rate, not val_loss
× seq_length alone.

**6. "Ready to deploy" is a dangerous phrase.**

It's a phrase that lets a sweep be marked "done" without the artifact
reaching production. Once an artifact is at the merged-safetensors
stage, the cost of one more step (`deploy.sh && curl health`) is
trivial compared to the cost of letting it rot. Either ship it or
delete it. The middle state — exists, ready, nobody using — is the
expensive state.

**7. Tie-breaker for finish order.**

The four variants finished in the order superclean (13:26) →
r128_short (12:18) → r128 (22:55) → bigseq (23:00). The first variant to
finish — r128_short — was the worst of the four. The last to finish was
the best. If the deployment pipeline had been "ship the first variant
that crosses a threshold," we'd have shipped the worst variant. Order
of completion is not order of quality; the gate has to be on the metric,
not on the wall clock.

**8. Generalizes: any sweep without graduation produces shelf-warmers.**

This is the rule that subsumes the other seven. A sweep is a generator
of candidates. Without an automatic, gate-based, time-bounded promotion
mechanism, the candidates accumulate and the median candidate that
*ships* is the one whose existence happened to coincide with a human
remembering to deploy it. The metric improvements that come out of the
sweep are real; the production-line improvements that come out of the
sweep are random. bigseq is the proof.

## Where this leaves the planner

The production planner is still serving `llama32-1b-epoch3-v3` at port
19320. `v3_r128` is live at 19305 as the rank-axis A/B reference. The
two losing variants (`v3_superclean`, `v3_r128_short`) were never
exported. `v3_bigseq` — best of sweep, 0.166 nats below the production
baseline — is fused, merged, and idle on cato. `v3_r256_short` never
existed.

The next sweep is a v4 grid that does pick up bigseq's seq=8192 as the
default and shifts the axes to LR schedule, curation threshold pairs,
and a longer cosine warmup. The graduation pipeline is the open work.
