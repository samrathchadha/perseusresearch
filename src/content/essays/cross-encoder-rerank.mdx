> tl;dr: A bi-encoder embeds query and document independently, so the
> retrieval step is an ANN lookup over pre-computed vectors. A
> cross-encoder reads `(query, doc)` jointly through a full transformer
> stack — quadratic in the concatenated sequence length, per pair.
> Reranking the top-100 of an ANN lookup with a cross-encoder is the
> standard cascade. Perseus ships it as an opt-in flag
> (`PERSEUS_RETRIEVAL_RERANK=1`, default off) because the
> retrieval-service already reranks inside `mode=hybrid`, the
> perseus-side second pass was never validated on our own corpus, and
> silent no-op behaviour bit us during the 2026-05 sweep thrash. The
> production reranker is Qwen3-Reranker-4B (CoIR nDCG@10 = 89.18);
> bge-reranker-v2-m3 was rejected at 35.97; jina-reranker-v3 lost
> 1,084 restart loops to a `JinaForRanking` architecture that doesn't
> exist in the pinned vLLM image.

## 1. The cascade

Retrieval is two questions composed. *Given a query, which 100
documents out of N are even plausible candidates?* That's question
one. *Given those 100, which 10 belong at the top?* That's question
two. The two questions have very different cost shapes, and the
right architecture for each is different.

A bi-encoder answers question one. The query is embedded once into a
$d$-dimensional vector; every document in the corpus is embedded
*once at index time* into the same space; retrieval is the
approximate-nearest-neighbour lookup of `query_vec` against the
indexed corpus. The per-query cost is $O(\log N)$ in the corpus size
under a competent ANN structure (HNSW, IVF-PQ), with a constant that
depends on $d$. The model never sees `(query, doc)` jointly.

A cross-encoder answers question two. For each of the $N$ candidate
documents, the model reads `[CLS] query [SEP] doc [SEP]` *as one
sequence* and emits a single relevance score from the `[CLS]` head.
The per-pair cost is the cost of one transformer forward pass over a
sequence of length $L = |q| + |d|$ — quadratic in $L$ inside each
attention layer, linear in the number of layers and in the model's
hidden width.

The asymmetry is load-bearing. Bi-encoder retrieval is cheap because
the document side is amortised across queries; the only on-line cost
is one query embed and one ANN lookup. Cross-encoder rerank is
expensive because the document side is *not* amortised — every query
re-embeds every candidate jointly with the query, and the rerank cost
is paid per query, per candidate.

## 2. The cost in numbers

Concretely:

- **Bi-encoder retrieval cost.** One query embed (one forward pass
  through Qwen3-Embedding-4B at $L \approx 32$ query tokens) plus an
  ANN lookup against the qdrant collection. The query embed dominates
  at fp16 on a V100 — call it ~50ms wall-clock for the 4B encoder
  over a short query, plus low-millisecond ANN lookup. *Document
  embeds are pre-computed at index time and never recomputed
  on-line.*
- **Cross-encoder rerank cost.** One forward pass through
  Qwen3-Reranker-4B per `(query, doc)` pair, with $L \approx 32 +
  \text{snippet length}$ tokens. Cross-encoder cost scales as $O(N
  L^2 d)$ across the $N$ candidates — the $L^2$ is the attention
  cost inside each layer; the $d$ is per-attention-head and per-
  layer. The retrieval-service ships with `RERANK_TOP_K_CANDIDATES =
  100` ([`crates/retrieval-service/src/config.rs:85`](https://github.com/starling-sh/perseus))
  — that's 100 forward passes through a 4B-parameter cross-encoder
  per query.

The headline ratio is: bi-encoder lookup is one forward pass on the
query side; cross-encoder rerank of the top-100 is one hundred
forward passes on `(query, doc)`-concatenated input. For
fixed-architecture rerankers the per-pair cost dominates the
end-of-pipeline latency budget, which is exactly why rerank is
gated behind a flag.

Per-pair attention cost is $O(L^2)$ in the concatenated sequence
length. For $L = 512$ (a comfortable snippet budget for codet5p-class
encoders) the attention block alone does ~262k pair-token
interactions per layer per head. The bi-encoder, over the same
snippet but without the joint pair, does $O(L)$ work per token —
self-attention is still $L^2$ inside the encoder, but the encoder
runs *once* on the document side at index time, not $N$ times per
query.

Two consequences:

1. Reranker latency is governed by $N$ and $L$, not by corpus size.
   Doubling the corpus does not double rerank cost; doubling
   `rerank_top_k` does.
2. There is a sweet spot for $N$. Below it, the reranker has too
   little to work with — the top-K it produces is just the top-K the
   bi-encoder already produced. Above it, you pay $L^2$ for
   diminishing recall gains. The Perseus default of 100 is the
   inherited Python-service value; **no in-house sweep over the
   rerank_top_k axis (50 / 100 / 200) has been logged**
   ([`HISTORY/30:352-353`](https://github.com/starling-sh/perseus)).

## 3. Why end-of-query rerank is off by default

`PERSEUS_RETRIEVAL_RERANK=1` is the planner-side knob that triggers a
second rerank pass *after* `build_hits` returns. The implementation
([`src/search/engine/config_types.rs:350`](https://github.com/starling-sh/perseus))
sends `(query, [snippets])` to `POST /v1/rerank`, reorders
`final_hits` by the returned indices, and overwrites `hit.score`
with the rerank score. Partial or out-of-range indices silently
preserve the original order — that's the no-op failure mode.

The default is `0`. Three reasons:

1. **The retrieval-service already reranks inside `mode=hybrid`.**
   The hybrid retriever
   ([`crates/retrieval-service/src/retriever/hybrid.rs:130`](https://github.com/starling-sh/perseus))
   does an RRF fuse of dense and sparse results, then reranks the top
   `rerank_candidates` (default 100). When perseus calls
   `hybrid_search` it is already getting a reranked list back. A
   second perseus-side rerank pass is *the same model on the same
   candidates with a tighter $N$* — at best a refinement of the same
   signal; at worst a contradictory reorder if the snippet text fed
   to the second pass differs from the first pass.
2. **It was never validated on our own corpus.** The reranker choice
   was made on Qwen3-Reranker-4B's published CoIR nDCG@10 = 89.18 and
   the operational rule that bge-reranker-v2-m3's 35.97 was
   unacceptable ([`HISTORY/30:374-375`](https://github.com/starling-sh/perseus)).
   No in-house head-to-head against gold patches has been run on
   either model. The single-source-of-truth retrieval quality table
   in `HISTORY/30` lists every row as "not measured" for in-house
   recall@k. Shipping a second rerank pass on top of an unmeasured
   first pass is double-blind.
3. **Silent no-op behaviour bit us.** Catalogued as risk B16 in
   `hi-compact.md:465` and `HISTORY/07_dead_ends.md:922`: "end-of-query
   rerank silently swaps top hits when `PERSEUS_RETRIEVAL_RERANK=1`"
   — measurable behaviour change, unmeasured direction. During the
   2026-05 sweep thrash the operational guidance was to *explicitly
   set `PERSEUS_RETRIEVAL_RERANK=0` and
   `PERSEUS_RETRIEVAL_MODE_DEFAULT=dense`* to neutralise rerank
   flakiness when qwen3-rerank was unhealthy
   ([`hi.md:9026 / 9095 / 9116 / 20782`](https://github.com/starling-sh/perseus)).

The flag exists. It's wired. It is off because turning it on costs
$N \cdot L^2$ ms per query and the recall delta has never been
measured on our own gold set.

## 4. Models tried

Four reranker candidates went through the pipeline. Three were
rejected; one is live.

**Qwen3-Reranker-4B — live.** Cross-encoder, 4B parameters, fp16 on
V100 served by vLLM at cato `:8060` as `qwen3-rerank` with
`--hf-overrides
'{"architectures":["Qwen3ForSequenceClassification"],
"classifier_from_token":["no","yes"],
"is_original_qwen3_reranker":true}'`
([`HISTORY/30:80-83`](https://github.com/starling-sh/perseus)). The
HF-override is load-bearing — without it vLLM's default architecture
resolver falls through to a generation head that does not exist on the
reranker checkpoint. CoIR nDCG@10 = **89.18** on the code corpus;
selected on published bench plus Apache-2.0 plus "fits 3 replicas
per V100 at fp16."

**bge-reranker-v2-m3 — rejected.** CoIR nDCG@10 = **35.97**
([`claude_revival.md:218`](https://github.com/starling-sh/perseus)).
Open-license alternative, but the 53-point gap to Qwen3 is
catastrophic. Not deployed.

**Qwen3-Reranker-0.6B — rejected.** Smaller variant of the same
architecture, "too small" per the selection log. No CoIR number cited
in the rejection.

**jina-reranker-v3 — rejected, expensively.** Tried first on the
2026-04-22 cato-101 rebuild. Published bench was higher than Qwen3.
Failure mode:

```
vllm/vllm-openai:latest
→ JinaForRanking architecture missing in pinned image
→ falls back to TransformersForCausalLM
→ lm_head.weight init fails
→ container restarts
→ ... × 1084
```

`PR #38800` was supposedly merged 2026-04-10 per upstream research,
but the pinned `:latest` digest did not contain it. Swapped to
Qwen3-Reranker-4B via the HF-override workaround. The standing rule
([`HISTORY/07_dead_ends.md:23`](https://github.com/starling-sh/perseus))
is: don't retry jina-v3 unless vLLM PR #38800 is verified merged in
the pinned digest. Adjacent miss: LiteLLM's `/rerank` expects
singular `query`; jina exposes `/score` with plural `queries` —
would have required a translator either way.

The Qwen3-Reranker-4B → 89.18 / bge-reranker-v2-m3 → 35.97 gap is the
one number that justifies the cascade at all. If the reranker
delivers 53 nDCG@10 points over the next-best candidate, the $O(L^2
N)$ tax buys real signal. If the gap had been five points the
cost-benefit would have looked very different.

## 5. The wire-shape alias shim

Two retrieval services have run in production. The Python
`infra/retrieval-service/server/*.py` (service_version 0.3.x → 0.4.0)
shipped first; the Rust port `crates/retrieval-service/` (service_version
0.5.0) replaced it during the 2026-04-24 audit-fix chain. They speak
*different wire shapes* for the same logical response.

Python 0.3.x `/rerank`:
```json
{"results": [{"index": 2, "relevance_score": 0.9},
             {"index": 0, "relevance_score": 0.1}]}
```

Rust port `/rerank`:
```json
{"indices": [2, 0], "scores": [0.9, 0.1]}
```

The perseus-side `BlockingRetrievalClient` needs to speak to either.
The fix is a custom `Deserialize` in
[`src/search/retrieval.rs:240-261`](https://github.com/starling-sh/perseus)
that accepts both shapes and flattens to one canonical struct:

```rust
struct RerankRespWire {
    #[serde(default)]
    indices: Vec<usize>,
    #[serde(default)]
    scores: Vec<f32>,
    #[serde(default)]
    results: Vec<RerankResultWire>,
}

struct RerankResultWire {
    index: usize,
    #[serde(default, alias = "score")]
    relevance_score: f32,
}

impl<'de> Deserialize<'de> for RerankResp {
    fn deserialize<D: serde::Deserializer<'de>>(d: D)
        -> Result<Self, D::Error>
    {
        let wire = RerankRespWire::deserialize(d)?;
        // Python shape: collapse `results` into parallel vectors.
        if !wire.results.is_empty() {
            let mut indices = Vec::with_capacity(wire.results.len());
            let mut scores = Vec::with_capacity(wire.results.len());
            for r in wire.results {
                indices.push(r.index);
                scores.push(r.relevance_score);
            }
            return Ok(RerankResp { indices, scores });
        }
        Ok(RerankResp { indices: wire.indices, scores: wire.scores })
    }
}
```

The embedding side has the same problem. Python returned `vectors`;
the Rust port returned `embeddings`. A simpler serde alias does the
job there:

```rust
pub(crate) struct EmbedResp {
    /// Accept both `embeddings` (Rust port contract) and `vectors`
    /// (Python service contract). Both shapes carry a `Vec<Vec<f32>>`
    /// — aliasing lets one client speak to either service unchanged.
    #[serde(default, alias = "vectors")]
    pub embeddings: Vec<Vec<f32>>,
}
```

Two unit tests pin both directions
([`src/search/retrieval.rs:1211-1232`](https://github.com/starling-sh/perseus)):
`rerank_resp_accepts_python_results_shape` round-trips `{"results":
[{"index": 2, "relevance_score": 0.9}, ...]}`;
`embed_resp_accepts_vectors_alias` round-trips `{"vectors":
[[0.1, 0.2], ...]}`. The alias shim was load-bearing during the
cutover — the Rust service was promoted to default endpoint the same
week it shipped, and any client still pointing at the Python service
(autoresearch, eval harnesses, the multi-bench driver) kept working
without recompilation.

This is the kind of compatibility layer that looks like over-engineering
in isolation and is mandatory in practice. The cost is twenty lines of
custom `Deserialize`; the benefit is that a service rewrite does not
break every caller.

## 6. When the cascade earns its keep

The cross-encoder rerank cascade is worth its $N \cdot L^2$ cost when
two conditions hold simultaneously.

**Condition one: the bi-encoder leaves real signal on the table.**
This happens when the query-doc semantic gap is too large for the
bi-encoder to bridge in $d$ dimensions. Symptoms: top-1 from the
bi-encoder is wrong but top-10 contains the right answer; queries
that paraphrase rather than quote the gold doc; queries that require
multi-hop reasoning over text the bi-encoder hasn't seen jointly.
HyDE (hypothetical-document expansion) is a *cheaper* version of the
same fix — synthesise a hallucinated doc that's lexically closer to
the gold doc, then bi-encode that. When HyDE is already on and the
top-100 still misses, the cross-encoder is the next lever.

**Condition two: the latency budget can absorb $N \cdot L^2$.** For
the Perseus planner loop a single retrieval call is one of dozens of
operations inside a multi-second MCTS expansion. An extra ~1–2s for
top-100 rerank is in-budget. For an interactive code-search box where
the user expects sub-300ms first-paint, top-100 rerank is out of
budget; the cascade truncates to top-20 rerank or skips rerank
entirely.

The flip side: when the bi-encoder is *already good enough* — when
the top-1 is right 95% of the time on the actual workload — the
reranker is paying $O(L^2 N)$ to reshuffle the same answer. For
ripgrep-style exact-match code search the bi-encoder over codet5p-110m
already lands the right file in top-3 most of the time; the
cross-encoder rerank is theatre.

The default-off posture is exactly this calculus. Until the in-house
recall@k sweep that compares bm25 / dense / hybrid / hybrid+rerank
against gold patches is logged, we know that:

- Qwen3-Reranker-4B beats bge-reranker-v2-m3 by 53 nDCG@10 points on
  public CoIR. (Reranker choice is correct.)
- The retrieval-service already reranks inside `mode=hybrid`. (One
  rerank pass is already in the budget.)
- A second perseus-side rerank pass on top of `mode=hybrid` has
  unknown direction. (Default off.)

What's open: an in-house recall@k sweep on the multi-bench gold-patch
set, holding the embedder fixed and toggling rerank on/off, with
$N \in \{50, 100, 200\}$ to find the actual sweet spot of the
candidate-set size. Without that, the flag stays off and the cascade
stays in the retrieval-service.
