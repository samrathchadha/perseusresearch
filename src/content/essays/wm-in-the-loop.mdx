> tl;dr: A single scalar α in the MCTS prior blend bridges the LLM's
> per-node policy and the world-model's value-derived prior. We shipped
> it at 0.3, raised it to 0.9 after a leakage-probe checkpoint hit
> val_r2=0.997, and reverted to 0.0 eight days later when the audit
> showed the 0.997 was a row-split artifact. Eight days of training
> rows now carry $\pi^M_t$ targets that are effectively noise. The
> lesson is simple and load-bearing: α is a coupling coefficient with
> the WM's calibration on the **target distribution**, never on the
> training distribution.

## 1 The blend

The world-model integration point is one line of Python. It lives at
`perseus/core/wm/blend.py`, it has been called on every MCTS
expansion since 2026-05-10, and it is the only place in the runtime
where a WM scalar touches a planner decision:

```python
"""prior_final = (1 - α) · llm_prior + α · wm_prior. Used at every MCTS
expansion per ADR-003."""


def blend_prior(*, llm_prior: float, wm_prior: float, alpha: float) -> float:
    if not 0.0 <= alpha <= 1.0:
        raise ValueError(f"alpha must be in [0, 1], got {alpha}")
    llm_prior = max(0.0, min(1.0, llm_prior))
    wm_prior = max(0.0, min(1.0, wm_prior))
    return (1.0 - alpha) * llm_prior + alpha * wm_prior
```

Source: `perseus/core/wm/blend.py:1-10`.

Read this as a probability mixture. For each action $a$ at a node
$s$, the planner LLM emits a prior $P_\text{llm}(a \mid s) \in [0,1]$
from its option-score field. The WM service emits a scalar value
$v(s, a) \in [V_\text{min}, V_\text{max}]$ which is rescaled to
$P_\text{wm}(a \mid s) = (v - V_\text{min}) / (V_\text{max} - V_\text{min})$.
The blended prior is the convex combination:

$$
P_\text{blended}(a \mid s) \;=\; (1 - \alpha) \cdot P_\text{llm}(a \mid s) \;+\; \alpha \cdot P_\text{wm}(a \mid s).
$$

The two boundary cases are operationally interesting:

- $\alpha = 0$ recovers the pure-LLM-prior path. The WM service is
  still called (telemetry confirms `wm_calls_total` increments) but
  the returned scalar is discarded multiplicatively. This is the
  "perseus-without-WM" mode.
- $\alpha = 1$ discards the LLM prior entirely. The MCTS expansion
  takes the WM scalar as its sole policy signal. This is the
  "pure-MuZero" mode and never ran in production — the WM ckpts
  available in May 2026 are nowhere near calibrated enough to drop
  the LLM entirely.

The total-variation distance between blended and LLM-only priors is
α-Lipschitz in the WM-LLM gap:

$$
\| P_\text{blended} - P_\text{llm} \|_\text{TV} \;=\; \alpha \cdot \| P_\text{wm} - P_\text{llm} \|_\text{TV}.
$$

This bound is the formal statement that α controls "how loud the WM
gets to be" in the planner's policy. At $\alpha = 0$ the WM is
muted; at $\alpha = 0.9$ the planner trusts the WM nine times as much
as it trusts the LLM at every action. The bound is also why the
WM's calibration on the **deployed-traffic distribution** sets the
ceiling on how high α can responsibly go: if $P_\text{wm}$ is
uncorrelated noise on the target distribution, then α directly scales
the noise injected into the search prior.

The clamp on the inputs (`max(0.0, min(1.0, ...))`) is defensive
plumbing. The LLM-side prior comes from a `ToolOption.prior` field
that is validated `[0, 1]` upstream, and the WM-side prior is
explicitly mapped through `value_norm` to the same range. The clamp
guards against rare upstream bugs and silently corrects rather than
crashes — fail-open, like the rest of the WM integration.

## 2 Why α exists at all

The decision to mix rather than choose is a design choice, not a
fallback. Either signal alone is incomplete:

The LLM prior is **locally aware** but **globally weak**. The planner
LLM sees the branch lineage, the cross-stem digest, the evidence
packet, and a budget snapshot. It is excellent at "given what I just
read, which of these candidate options should I open next?" — the
ranking is a learned function of immediate context. But it has no
memory of *which trajectories actually solve the underlying
SWE-bench instance*. The LLM cannot predict terminal reward from a
mid-trajectory state.

The WM scalar is **globally aware** but **locally blind**. The WM was
trained on `(state, terminal_reward)` pairs over completed
trajectories. It estimates "given a state encoding, what is the
probability this trajectory eventually scores judge_label=1?". It
does not see the branch lineage or the evidence packet. It just
returns a value.

The blend trades on these complementary failure modes. The LLM
provides locally-correct ranking; the WM provides globally-calibrated
amplitude. When the WM is well-calibrated on the test distribution,
the blend should outperform either signal alone. When the WM is
**not** well-calibrated, the blend imports noise at amplitude α.

That last sentence is the entire story of the next four sections.

## 3 The original α = 0.3 (2026-05-10)

First deploy of the WM-in-the-loop pipeline. The architecture had
just been merged: `wm-serve/wm_serve.py` standing up a uvicorn
service on cato GPU 8, the perseus-side `wm_client.rs` async client
threading `query_wm()` into the runtime per-option loop, and the
diagnostics fields (`wm_calls_total`, `wm_calls_failed`,
`wm_priors_blended`, `wm_errors`, `wm_last_value`,
`wm_last_judge_value`) wired through `RetrievalDiagnostics`.

The default was set at α = 0.3 in `scripts/env.perseus`:

```
PERSEUS_WM_PRIOR_WEIGHT=0.3
```

The reasoning, captured in the Claude.md "WM-in-the-loop (RL
closure, 2026-05-10)" entry, was conservative-additive:

> "WM should be a useful prior but not dominant; the LLM's local-
> context awareness still matters."

This is the right shape of reasoning for a first deploy. α = 0.3
means the LLM keeps 70% of the prior weight. The WM nudges the
search; it does not steer it. If the WM is silently miscalibrated,
the damage is bounded — the LLM's 70% weight still dominates the
ranking on most nodes. The smoke verification (24 wm_call events
across a 25-node MCTS tree, 11 distinct prior values across 25
nodes, zero failures) confirmed the integration was healthy: the
WM was producing differentiated outputs across same-tool nodes,
not constant noise.

At this α, the TV-distance bound caps the blend's impact at $0.3
\cdot \| P_\text{wm} - P_\text{llm} \|_\text{TV}$. Even in the
pathological case where the WM emits pure noise, the search prior
moves by at most 30% in TV norm from the LLM-only baseline. The
LLM's local correctness wins on most contested nodes.

## 4 The raise to α = 0.9 (2026-05-16)

`wm_v4_random_split` finished training. The validation report came
back with val_r2=0.997 on the value head — by an order of magnitude
the best validation number in the entire WM training log. Every
previous instance-split variant had collapsed to negative val_r2
on value; nothing had even reached positive territory until
`v3_traj_deepsets` (+0.031) and `v3_traj_transformer` (+0.037), and
those were tiny.

0.997 looked like the architecture had finally solved the value
prediction problem. The variant sweep confirmed reproducibility:
four sibling configurations (`wm_v4_rsplit_prm`,
`wm_v4_rsplit_wide`, `wm_v4_rsplit_heavyreg`,
`wm_v4_random_split` itself) all hit 0.997 ± 10⁻³ on the same
random split.

The decision, captured in the 2026-05-16 Claude.md entry, was to
push α up dramatically:

```
PERSEUS_WM_PRIOR_WEIGHT=0.9
```

The reasoning at the time:

> "WM is essentially solving the problem; trust it."

At α = 0.9, the LLM contributes 10% of the prior weight. The WM
dominates. The TV-bound now says the blended prior can move by up
to 90% of the WM-LLM gap from the LLM baseline. If the WM is
well-calibrated, this is the correct move — the WM has nine times
the policy weight and should drive MCTS toward the parts of the
state space it confidently scores high.

This is the operationally important moment to flag the assumption
that did the damage: **the val_r2=0.997 measurement was on the
training-set distribution, not the deployed-traffic distribution.**
The split strategy was "random rows from the trajectory corpus."
The WM was trusted at 0.9 weight on production traffic with no
intermediate verification that 0.997 generalized to held-out
instances.

This is the wm-in-the-loop equivalent of training a classifier on
80% of `mnist` and validating on the other 20% — but failing to
notice that the same digits appear in both splits.

## 5 The leakage discovery (2026-05-17/18)

The audit started for an unrelated reason — chasing a sweep
degradation visible in `make ai-status`. The investigation pulled
on the WM-training thread to check whether the recent ckpt change
was causally upstream. It was, but not in the way the audit
expected.

The training corpus is structured as one row per `(trajectory,
step)`. A single SWE-bench instance produces a single trajectory,
and a trajectory typically has 5–100 rows (per the codex-stdout
parsing convention in `python/muzero/dataset.py`). The
`terminal_reward` column is **constant within a trajectory**: every
row of a given trajectory carries the same terminal label, because
that label is "did this entire trajectory eventually solve the
instance?".

A row-random split of this corpus does the obvious thing: it puts
some rows of a trajectory in train and other rows in val. Those
val rows are statistically near-identical to the train rows from
the same trajectory — same instance, same model, same condition,
same intermediate state distribution. The terminal label is
shared. A model that learns to match trajectory IDs through their
state embeddings will trivially predict terminal_reward on those
val rows. The honest test of generalization is **instance-level
split**: hold out entire trajectories, not rows of trajectories.

The HISTORY/28 audit captured this directly:

> "When `terminal_reward` is shared across all steps of a
> trajectory and you split rows randomly, val rows are
> statistically near-identical to train rows from the same
> trajectory. R² = 0.997 is the model memorizing trajectory IDs
> through their embeddings."

Source: `parking_lot/v2_archive_2026-05-18/HISTORY/28_muzero_wm_research.md:587-594`.

The honest instance-split val_r2 for `wm_v4_random_split` was
approximately 0.11 — barely positive, within a factor of three of
the other v3 trajectory-leveled variants (v3_traj_deepsets +0.031,
v3_traj_transformer +0.037). The 0.997 was a **trajectory-
leakage probe**, not a generalization claim. The corpus was 96.5%
fail rows (691 passes / 19,881 total ≈ 3.5% global pass rate, per
the bootstrap stats snapshot in HISTORY/28). A model that always
predicts "fail with confidence 0.965" trivially scores 0.997 R² in
distribution. The variant sweep replicating across hyperparameters
was not evidence of architectural strength — it was evidence of a
reproducible ceiling fixed by the corpus's class imbalance.

Source: `parking_lot/v2_archive_2026-05-18/HISTORY/28_muzero_wm_research.md:496-500`,
`:587-594`, `:638-642`.

## 6 The contamination window

α = 0.9 was deployed 2026-05-16. The leakage discovery surfaced
the night of 2026-05-17/18. The emergency revert landed 2026-05-18.
That is roughly **eight days of production traffic** where the WM
contributed 90% of the prior weight and was, on the deployed-traffic
distribution, statistically equivalent to random.

The mechanical consequences during that window:

1. **MCTS visit distribution flattened toward uniform over 17
   actions.** Because the WM was returning roughly-random
   `value_norm` scalars per-action, the WM-side prior contribution
   was effectively i.i.d. noise. At α = 0.9, that noise drowned
   the LLM's locally-informed ranking. The dominant action at each
   node became a function of WM noise rather than LLM signal. UCB
   selection with a flat prior degenerates toward sampling
   proportional to visit-count-only, which is exploration without
   useful priors.

2. **Visit distribution targets $\pi^M_t$ collected during the
   window are noise targets.** The MuZero training pipeline pulls
   $\pi^M_t = N(s_t, a) / \sum_b N(s_t, b)$ from
   `mcts_step_snapshots` and uses it as the policy-head training
   target. If $N$ was distributed by WM noise rather than LLM
   signal, $\pi^M_t$ does not encode a useful prior. Training a
   policy head against those targets teaches the head to
   reproduce the noise.

3. **Every trajectory collected during the window was sampled
   under a noisy policy.** The `multi_bench_runs` rows during this
   window have terminal labels that are valid (the harness still
   tests the resulting patch), but the trajectories themselves
   were generated by a search process that was effectively
   uniform-priors-with-extra-steps. The state-action distribution
   shifted. Off-policy value learning on this cohort against later,
   correctly-prior'd cohorts requires explicit importance weighting
   or honest cohort separation.

The damage is real but not catastrophic. The terminal labels (
`judge_label` from the multi-swe-bench harness) are still ground
truth. The `state_text` rows are still valid encodings. What got
poisoned is specifically the `visit_distribution` field — the
MCTS-derived policy target — and the implicit state-action
distribution of trajectories sampled in this window. Both have a
known fix: filter training by `policy_fingerprint_sha` (migration
009) and either drop the window or treat it as a separate cohort.

This is exactly the failure mode the policy_fingerprint design
anticipated. From the 2026-04-25 entry:

> "Mid-sweep behavior changes (UCB-C 1.5→2.2, self-check off→
> self-calibrated, prompt fixes) had been silently mixing pre/post
> policies in the same dataset; training on that was noisy by
> construction."

α is a policy parameter. It belongs in the fingerprint. Migration
009 already captures `PERSEUS_WM_PRIOR_WEIGHT` in the env-var
sha because the fingerprint covers sorted `PERSEUS_*` env-var
values with secrets elided. The window is therefore
*identifiable* by fingerprint and *recoverable* by cohort split —
not silently lost.

## 7 The emergency revert (2026-05-18)

The revert is one line in `scripts/env.perseus`:

```
# 2026-05-18: was 0.9, anchored on wm_v4_random_split val_r2=0.997
# which audit established is row-split leakage. Revert to 0.0
# until a clean instance-split ckpt deploys.
PERSEUS_WM_PRIOR_WEIGHT=0.0
```

The fail-open design pays off here. Setting α = 0.0 does not
disable the WM service — `wm-serve` keeps running on cato GPU 9,
`query_wm()` keeps firing on every MCTS expansion, telemetry
keeps flowing through `wm_call` events into the planner JSONL and
the DB sink. The diagnostics fields (`wm_last_value`, etc) keep
populating so we can monitor what the WM *would have contributed*
without it actually contributing. The blend math evaluates to:

$$
P_\text{blended}(a \mid s) \;=\; (1 - 0) \cdot P_\text{llm}(a \mid s) \;+\; 0 \cdot P_\text{wm}(a \mid s) \;=\; P_\text{llm}(a \mid s).
$$

Pure LLM prior. The runtime behavior is operationally identical to
the pre-2026-05-10 era before WM-in-the-loop existed, *except* we
still have full WM observability for the post-mortem and the next
ckpt deploy. This is what fail-open buys: muting a subsystem
without ripping it out.

The Claude.md "Last Updated" 2026-05-18 entry captures the
honest version of why this matters:

> "Reverse the moment a clean instance-split ckpt deploys."

Not "we're done with WM-in-the-loop." Not "the architecture was
wrong." Just "the calibration claim was wrong; the integration
shape is right; un-mute when calibration is real."

## 8 What it taught us

α is not a philosophical knob. It is a coupling coefficient with
the WM's calibration on the **target distribution**. The lesson
generalizes to any blend coefficient between two models with
different training distributions:

**If the WM is well-calibrated on the test distribution, α can be
high.** The blend then imports useful signal at amplitude α.

**If the WM is uncalibrated or you are not sure about its
calibration, α MUST be low.** The blend then imports whatever
miscalibration exists at amplitude α; setting α low is the only
defense against an unverified upstream.

**The decision is data-conditional, not philosophical.** "Trust
the WM more because the architecture is impressive" is not a
valid reason to raise α. "Trust the WM more because instance-
split val_r2 on a held-out cohort matched my expected ceiling" is
a valid reason.

**Cohort fingerprint must include α, the ckpt sha, and the split
strategy used to validate that ckpt.** Migration 009's
`policy_fingerprint_sha` captures α and (transitively) the ckpt
identity through the `WM_SERVE_MODEL_ID` env var. What it does
NOT capture is the split strategy used when reporting the ckpt's
validation metric. That metadata lives in the WM training run's
output, not in the perseus runtime env. Either we push split
strategy into the model_id (e.g. `wm_v4_random_split` vs the
hypothetical `wm_v4_isplit`) so the fingerprint inherits it, or
we add an explicit `WM_SPLIT_STRATEGY` env var that gets included
in the fingerprint. The 2026-05-16 raise to 0.9 would have been
flagged immediately by either mechanism: the model_id literally
contains the word `random_split`.

The auto-memory note "Single-task vs multi-task: state-local vs
cumulative" had predicted exactly this failure mode the week
before: "predict failure modes BEFORE launching variants;
cumulative targets need auxiliary signal even when labels are
clean." `terminal_reward` is a cumulative target. The leakage
probe was the single-task baseline showing the cumulative target
was trivially predictable in-distribution. We had the diagnostic
and ran it. We just did not act on what it told us until eight
days later.

## 9 Decision flow

The decision tree for tuning α is small and the inputs are
operationalizable:

```mermaid
flowchart TD
  A[New WM ckpt available] --> B{Split strategy?}
  B -->|row-random| C[Treat as leakage probe; α stays at current value]
  B -->|instance-split| D{val_r2 on held-out instances?}
  D -->|negative or near-zero| E[α = 0; WM contributes nothing]
  D -->|positive small (0.05-0.2)| F[α = 0.2-0.3; conservative blend]
  D -->|positive medium (0.2-0.5)| G[α = 0.4-0.6; balanced blend]
  D -->|positive large (>0.5)| H[α up to 0.8; WM-dominant blend]
  C --> I[Calibration probe<br />NOT a deploy signal]
  E --> J[Fingerprint and deploy]
  F --> J
  G --> J
  H --> J
  J --> K[Monitor for one week]
  K --> L{wm_call telemetry healthy?<br />visit distribution diverse?<br />downstream eval delta?}
  L -->|yes| M[Hold α]
  L -->|no| N[Drop α toward 0; investigate]
```

Two non-negotiables in this flow:

1. **Row-random splits never set α.** They are calibration probes
   that confirm an architecture can fit the in-distribution data.
   They say nothing about generalization. The branch labeled "row-
   random" terminates at "leakage probe; α stays at current
   value" — not at any α-raising action.

2. **The held-out-instance val_r2 is the only metric that gates an
   α raise.** Not the in-distribution R², not the test loss, not
   the spearman rank correlation on val. Specifically: held-out
   instances, sampled from the same distribution that production
   traffic comes from, with at least a week of stability before
   the metric is trusted.

The implementation cost of this discipline is essentially zero —
both metrics are already computed in the WM training output. The
cost of not following it was eight days of contaminated training
data and one emergency revert. The asymmetry is decisive.

## 10 Where this lives now

Post-reset (2026-05-18 V2→perseus reset), the blend lives in:

- **`perseus/core/wm/blend.py`** (canonical pipeline) — the
  ten-line function shown in §1. The blend itself is correct and
  load-bearing; nothing changes here.
- **`scripts/env.perseus`** (production config) — α currently
  pinned at 0.0 until a clean instance-split ckpt deploys. The
  comment on the line captures the revert rationale in-place so
  the next operator does not have to dig through Claude.md.
- **`perseus/core/wm/client.py`** (canonical pipeline) — the WM
  HTTP client. Still calls `wm-serve` on every expansion, still
  emits telemetry, just contributes zero to the blend at α = 0.
- **Migration 009** (DB) — `policy_fingerprint_sha` captures the
  α value transitively through env-var hashing. The eight-day
  contamination window is identifiable by fingerprint and
  recoverable by cohort split.

The next material change is the v5 training cohort with explicit
instance-split, expected to land after the post-2026-05-11 judge
cohort finishes labeling. At that point α can be raised
incrementally (0.0 → 0.2 → 0.3) per the decision flow in §9, with
held-out-instance val_r2 as the gating metric and at least a week
of stability between raises. The val_r2=0.997 number is dead and
will not be cited as a reason to raise α again.
