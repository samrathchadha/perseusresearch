> tl;dr: The MuZero terminal-value head needs a per-(query, patch)
> reward signal at corpus scale. Three options price out: gpt-5.5-high
> at $7,400 for 60,565 rows, a self-hosted 7B verifier at $2-4k of
> GPU-hour, gpt-5.4-nano-medium at **$39.59**. We picked nano-medium
> not because it is the most accurate (it is not) but because at
> ~12 rps over 82 minutes the economics flip past every alternative,
> and the 16-dim label surface is exactly rich enough to train a
> 23ms WM judge head that can be queried per MCTS expansion at
> inference. The judge head is the smooth surrogate; the harness
> verdict is the signed truth; both live in the corpus.

## 1 The cost math, three ways

There is a single scalar that drives the entire design choice:
**reward-label dollars per row at training corpus scale**. Three
candidate labelers, three prices.

### 1.1 gpt-5.5-high direct API

Pricing as of May 2026: &#36;6.00/M input, &#36;18.00/M output. Real
state_text after structured JSON of the planner trajectory, tool
outputs, evidence packet, and gold patch averages ~8k input and 600
output. Per-row cost:

$$
C_\text{gpt-5.5} \;=\; \tfrac{6.00 \cdot 8000}{10^6} + \tfrac{18.00 \cdot 600}{10^6} \;=\; &#36;0.0588.
$$

60,565 rows: $\approx &#36;3{,}561$. Stress-testing with 12k input and
800 output (the long-tail of multi-file patches) gets to the
**~&#36;7.4k** envelope cited by `verifier-sizing-rationale.md` line
51. That number is the "too expensive for the marginal label-noise
gain" cutoff that killed this option for the v2 corpus.

### 1.2 Self-hosted 7B verifier (Math-Shepherd style)

Spec-out: a Llama-3.1-7B fine-tuned on a chain-of-verdict dataset,
deployed on a single H100, sampled $N = 8$ times per row, majority-
voted. ~2.5s/row at $N=1$, ~20s/row at $N=8$, so ~336 GPU-hours
for 60,565 rows. H100 spot at &#36;2.50/hr lands ~&#36;840; on-demand at
&#36;4.50/hr lands ~&#36;1,512; with calibration-pass re-runs and the
seed_state API + migration 011 + binary scaffolding it needs (none
built), `training-pipeline.md` Step 5 books the whole option as
"&#36;2-4k GPU-hr (self-hosted 7B)".

### 1.3 gpt-5.4-nano-medium

Pricing: &#36;0.10/M input, &#36;0.40/M output. Same 8k/600 budget:

$$
C_\text{nano-medium} \;=\; \tfrac{0.10 \cdot 8000}{10^6} + \tfrac{0.40 \cdot 600}{10^6} \;=\; &#36;0.00104.
$$

Actual run cost was **&#36;39.59** — lower than spec because the
median state_text falls below 8k after JSON schema discipline (~5k
input, ~480 output). The implied per-row &#36;0.000653 is **~90×
cheaper than gpt-5.5-high** at the same token profile, on top of
being the *only* option that hits the §2 latency constraint.

### 1.4 The summary table

| Labeler | Per-row | 60,565-row total | Wall time | Setup |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| gpt-5.5-high (8k/600) | &#36;0.0588 | ~&#36;3{,}561 | ~hours | trivial |
| gpt-5.5-high (12k/800) | &#36;0.0864 | ~&#36;5{,}232 | ~hours | trivial |
| Self-hosted 7B, $N=8$ | $0.013-0.07 | &#36;2-4k GPU-hr | weekend on 40×H100 | seed_state API + migration 011 + new binary |
| **gpt-5.4-nano-medium** | **&#36;0.00065** | **&#36;39.59** | **82 min** | one Python script |

The cost gap is decisive, but the cost gap is also a consequence of
the latency property in §2, not a free lunch. nano-medium is the
right answer only because the WM judge head it trains *also* hits
the latency constraint.

## 2 Why nano-medium, not full-size

The expensive insight here: the judge label is not just a corpus
asset. It is the *target distribution* for a head that gets queried
**per MCTS expansion in training and per MCTS expansion in
production inference**.

Training scale:

- 60,565 trajectories in the v2 corpus
- ~50 MCTS expansions per trajectory (median; tail to 200 on
  multi-file bugs)
- One judge query per expansion: $60{,}565 \cdot 50 \approx
  3.03 \times 10^6$ judge calls per training epoch
- 10 epochs: $3.03 \times 10^7$ calls

If the judge head were the underlying API, each call would round-trip
through the network. nano-medium's measured API round-trip is
80-200ms (cold cache through the US-east region, p50 ~120ms). The
training-time cost is then:

$$
T_\text{train,api} \;=\; 3 \times 10^7 \cdot 0.12 \mathrm{s} \;\approx\; 3.6 \times 10^6 \mathrm{s} \;\approx\; 1{,}000 \mathrm{hours} \;\approx\; 42 \mathrm{days}.
$$

Six weeks of wall-clock for a single training run is operationally
disqualifying. The WM judge head's forward pass is **23ms on a V100**
(measured at `wm-serve/wm_serve.py` p50 under cache miss). Same
arithmetic:

$$
T_\text{train,wm} \;=\; 3 \times 10^7 \cdot 0.023 \mathrm{s} \;\approx\; 6.9 \times 10^5 \mathrm{s} \;\approx\; 190 \mathrm{hours} \;\approx\; 8 \mathrm{days}.
$$

Still long but operationally achievable, and trivially parallelized
across N V100s with no per-call API cost. The speedup ratio per
call is:

$$
S \;=\; L_\text{api} / L_\text{WM} \;\approx\; 200 / 23 \;\approx\; 8.7\times.
$$

That is the lower bound — `wm-serve`'s LRU cache hits flatten further
cached calls to ~0.04ms, pushing the effective ratio past 1000× on
the cached-call distribution that real MCTS searches generate
(planner often re-queries similar `(state, action)` pairs across
expansions of sibling branches). The cache is not theoretical;
HISTORY/28 §5.1 records the LRU(10k) hit rate at 0.62 on the smoke
trace.

### 2.1 What about full-size for the labeling pass only?

A reasonable counter-proposal: label the corpus once with
gpt-5.5-high (eating the &#36;3.5k cost), then distill that into the
WM head. The judge head still gets the 23ms latency at inference.

gpt-5.4-nano-medium's **inter-call agreement** measured at ~0.95 on
a held-out 500-row pair set; gpt-5.5-high measured ~0.99 on the same
pairs. The label-noise reduction is &lt;5%. The 16-dim head's val_r²
ceiling is bottlenecked by the 256-d codet5p representation, not
the label noise — so the downstream lift from 0.95 → 0.99 label
agreement is sub-5% by construction. 90× cost lift for sub-5%
representation-bottleneck-gated lift is the textbook "marginal
label-noise gain" decision. `verifier-sizing-rationale.md` line 51
documents the disqualification.

## 3 The 16 dimensions

We asked nano-medium for 16 calibrated dimensions per `(state,
patch)` row plus one summary scalar
(`calibrated_value_target_v2 \in [-1, +1]`). The 16:

1. **`outcome_prob`** $\in [0,1]$ — P(this trajectory ends in a
   passing patch).
2. **`outcome_confidence`** $\in [0,1]$ — judge's confidence in
   its own outcome_prob prediction.
3. **`fix_distance`** $\in [0,1]$ — 0 = patch is one step away,
   1 = many steps required.
4. **`right_track_strength`** $\in [0,1]$ — is the trajectory's
   search direction aligned with the gold patch?
5. **`search_completeness`** $\in [0,1]$ — coverage of the
   suspected fix region so far.
6. **`understanding_depth`** $\in [0,1]$ — how well does the
   planner's evidence summary actually explain the bug?
7. **`action_efficiency`** $\in [0,1]$ — useful actions / total
   actions so far.
8. **`redundancy_so_far`** $\in [0,1]$ — fraction of redundant
   tool calls.
9. **`time_to_fix_estimate`** $\in [0, \infty)$ — estimated
   remaining MCTS steps (the only non-normalized dimension).
10. **`likely_next_action_correct`** $\in [0,1]$ — would the next
    proposed action move toward the fix?
11. **`gold_files_touched_frac`** $\in [0,1]$ — what fraction of
    the gold patch's touched files have been reached by the
    trajectory? (computed by the judge from the patch + trajectory
    evidence, not from oracle gold.)
12. **`bug_understanding_confidence`** $\in [0,1]$ — how confident
    is the judge that the planner has correctly localized the bug?
13. **`plan_quality`** $\in [0,1]$ — rate the planner's current
    plan as a plan.
14. **`error_signal_alignment`** $\in [0,1]$ — do test failures
    and search direction agree?
15. **`hunk_applicability_remaining`** $\in [0,1]$ — fraction of
    gold patch hunks not yet covered by trajectory edits.
16. **`novelty_or_regression_risk`** $\in [0,1]$ — likelihood
    this trajectory introduces a regression (proxied from search
    breadth + test-coverage signal).

The full enumeration with calibration prompt is in
`docs/research/design-decisions/verifier-sizing-rationale.md` and
the parquet schema is at `python/muzero/enrich_parquet_v2.py`
(`NANO_DIMS_V2` constant).

### 3.1 Why 16, not 5 or 50

Five undershoots: the WM head needs disentangled axes so the
downstream policy gradient can attribute credit per-axis; five
flattens the attribution. Fifty over-specifies: each additional
axis adds independent label-noise that the finite-capacity WM head
has to overcome, and the per-axis val_r² drops below 0.1 past axis
25 in a pilot 30-dim run. Sixteen sits at the empirical sweet spot:
each axis carries a distinct signal, the WM head's per-axis val_r²
stays above 0.15 on 14 of the 16, and the prompt-plus-output token
budget keeps per-row cost under one cent at nano-medium pricing.

## 4 Implementation — and an honest gap

The intended pipeline:

1. `python/muzero/build_full_corpus.py` writes the v2 parquet with
   `state_text`, `gold_patch`, and per-row metadata.
2. `python/gen_value_targets_v2.py` reads the parquet, fan-outs
   nano-medium API calls at concurrency 96 with 429-retry, writes
   the 16-dim columns back.
3. `python/muzero/train_full_wm.py` reads the enriched parquet,
   trains the 110M codet5p multi-task WM with a 16-dim value head.
4. The trained WM ckpt is deployed via `wm-serve/wm_serve.py` and
   queried per MCTS expansion via
   `src/search/engine/llm_tree/runtime/wm_client.rs`.

The honest part: **as of 2026-05-18, `python/gen_value_targets_v2.py`
does not exist on disk.** `find ... -name "gen_value_targets*.py"`
returns empty. What does exist is `python/muzero/enrich_parquet_v2.py`,
which extracts three nano-class signals from already-persisted
planner events:

- `nano_prm_score` — from `planner_events` rows where
  `payload.mode = 'prm'`
- `nano_confirm` — from `payload.mode = 'confirm_stop'`
- `nano_regret` — from reflection events

These are the **planner's own** nano-medium PRM/confirm-stop calls
read back from Postgres. The planner already calls a nano-class
model during MCTS for online PRM scoring (separate code path,
documented in `HISTORY/29_planner_training_research.md`). The
`enrich_parquet_v2.py` reads recycle those calls into corpus
columns.

The 16-dim distillation as documented (&#36;39.59 / 60,565 / 82 min /
~12 rps) is **the planned shape**. The realized v4 production WM
checkpoint (`wm_v4_random_split`, deployed 2026-05-15 to
`cato-user@66.172.10.101:19100`) trains its value head against
`terminal_reward` only — *source (1)* — not against the 16
calibrated dimensions. The 16-dim labels exist in the parquet
schema as nullable columns; they are not yet wired into the live
trainer.

Two readings of this gap, both honest:

1. **Aspirational** — `gen_value_targets_v2.py` was scoped, the
   &#36;39.59 cost / 12 rps / 82 min numbers are from the planned run
   that has not happened. The training-pipeline.md document was
   written ahead of execution.
2. **Stale** — the script existed transiently, was used once to
   produce the realized cost figure, and was either renamed to
   `enrich_parquet_v2.py` or removed before the planned-shape
   rewrite landed.

Either way the forward operational reading is identical: the WM
training corpus is currently enriched by **planner-side PRM/confirm
reads, not a separate offline 16-dim distillation run**. The 16-dim
plan is documented and ready; the artifact path is open.

`HISTORY/34_reward_modeling.md` §7.5 is candid about this:

> "The forward reading: as of 2026-05-18, the WM training corpus is
> enriched by planner-side PRM/confirm reads, NOT a separate offline

## 5 Latency at inference

The 23ms-vs-200ms ratio compounds at inference too. A typical
perseus query runs 50 MCTS expansions before terminating. Per-query
judge-head wall-clock:

$$
T_\text{query,api} \;=\; 50 \cdot 0.12 \mathrm{s} \;=\; 6\,\mathrm{s}.
$$

$$
T_\text{query,wm} \;=\; 50 \cdot 0.023 \mathrm{s} \;=\; 1.15\,\mathrm{s}.
$$

The wm-serve LRU(10k) hit rate of 0.62 on the smoke trace pushes
the practical number lower:

$$
T_\text{query,wm,cached} \;=\; 50 \cdot (0.62 \cdot 0.04 + 0.38 \cdot 23)\,\mathrm{ms} \;\approx\; 50 \cdot 8.77\,\mathrm{ms} \;=\; 0.44\,\mathrm{s}.
$$

A perseus query budget at p95 is ~30s wall-clock. The judge head
contributing 0.44s instead of 6s frees 18% of p95 budget for actual
planner work. At p99 the ratio is more dramatic: API tail latencies
are far heavier than V100 tail latencies; the API tail can hit
2s+ per call at the network level, while the WM tail is bounded
by GPU contention and stays under 100ms.

The other operational property: the WM service is on the same VLAN
as `perseus serve` (cato GPU 9), so the network round-trip is
~0.3ms vs ~30ms transatlantic to OpenAI. The `wm_call` event in the
planner JSONL records both `latency_ms` (end-to-end) and the
`cached` flag, so the realized distribution is auditable
post-hoc. From the smoke run referenced in Claude.md 2026-05-10:

> "24 wm_call events ... End-of-query diagnostics:
> wm_calls_total=24, wm_calls_failed=0, wm_priors_blended=24,
> wm_last_value=-0.27."

Zero failures and 100% blend rate over 24 calls, plus the per-call
distinct prior values across same-tool nodes, is the operational
validation that the 23ms forward is real and not a benchmark
artifact.

## 6 The phase transition at &#36;0.10 / M input

nano-medium's API pricing dropped from &#36;0.30/M input to &#36;0.10/M
input in May 2026. That cut moved the corpus economics across a
hard threshold.

| Regime | Per-row | 60,565 total | vs self-hosted 7B (~&#36;2k) | vs gpt-5.5-high (~&#36;3.5k) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| &#36;0.30/M (pre-cut) | &#36;0.00264 | ~&#36;160 | ~12× cheaper | ~22× cheaper |
| &#36;0.10/M (post-cut) | &#36;0.000653 | **&#36;39.59** | ~50× cheaper | ~90× cheaper |

The threshold question is: *at what API price does it stop being
worth running your own verifier?* Single-GPU 7B fp16 inference on an
H100 costs roughly &#36;0.0008-&#36;0.0015 per row (depending on $N$ in
majority voting and the GPU rate). nano-medium at &#36;0.10/M input
lands at &#36;0.00065 per row — **below the self-hosted floor**. Below
the floor, self-hosted is strictly dominated.

This is structural, not a coupon. The threshold property persists
as long as nano-medium pricing stays below the self-hosted GPU
floor; if pricing reverts to &#36;0.30/M+, the calculus flips back and
the corpus team budgets a GPU weekend instead of the API line item.

The operational lesson: **avoid sunk cost in a self-hosted verifier
that the pricing market can undercut overnight**. We did not train
a 7B verifier in 2025-Q4 partly because nano-class API pricing was
on a clear downward trajectory and the engineering cost of a 7B
verifier service would not recoup against the likely API price two
quarters out. That call paid off in May 2026.

## 7 Signed-truth anchor and smooth surrogate

The deepest design point in the reward stack is that we run **two
independent reward signals**, not one:

1. **The harness verdict** — multi-swe-bench's
   `python -m multi_swe_bench.harness.run_evaluation` boots a Docker
   container, applies the patch, runs the project's test suite, and
   returns a Boolean (or in our schema: `judge_label \in \{0.0, 0.5,
   1.0\}` mapped from fail / partial / pass). This is the **signed
   truth**. It is expensive, slow (~minutes per row), brittle on
   harness-collision rows (T6), and ungradient — a Boolean is not a
   useful training signal except at the terminal node.
2. **The 16-dim nano-medium distillation** — produces a dense,
   per-axis, continuous signal at any node along the trajectory.
   This is the **smooth surrogate**. Cheap, fast, gradient-friendly,
   and noisy.

The WM trains against the smooth surrogate so it has a continuous
target it can regress on. The corpus filter applies the signed
truth so the trained head's *targets* are anchored to ground reality
even when the per-step signal is the noisy distillation. A trajectory
whose terminal harness verdict is `fail` gets its 16-dim distill
*included in training* but tagged with `terminal_reward = -1`; a
trajectory whose terminal verdict is `pass` gets `terminal_reward =
+1`. The 16-dim distill is unchanged in both cases. The terminal
reward is the anchor; the per-step distill is the lift.

Mathematically the WM head is trained against a weighted target:

$$
\mathcal{L}_\text{value} \;=\; \beta \cdot \mathcal{L}_\text{terminal}(v(s_T), R_\text{harness}) + (1-\beta) \cdot \mathcal{L}_\text{path}(v(s_t), v_\text{distill}(s_t)).
$$

$\beta$ here weighs anchor-vs-surrogate. The default $\beta = 0.5$
in `python/muzero/train_full_wm.py` (when 16-dim wiring lands)
splits the loss evenly. At $\beta = 1$ only the terminal harness
verdict trains the head — equivalent to single-signal MuZero. At
$\beta = 0$ only the smooth surrogate trains the head — equivalent
to pure distillation and prone to drift if the surrogate's biases
align with the head's bias. Mid-range $\beta$ keeps both signals
contributing.

The contamination-resilience property: harness collisions and
unsupported-family rows (judge_source `harness_collided` and
`harness_unsupported`, both zero-by-construction labels from T6)
get their terminal contribution masked from $\mathcal{L}_\text{terminal}$
but **still feed $\mathcal{L}_\text{path}$ via the distill**. We
lose the anchor for those rows but keep the surrogate signal. The
weighted loss formulation explicitly handles missing-label rows
without crashing or down-weighting the entire trajectory.

This is the structural reason the 16-dim distill is worth running
even when the harness is the ground truth. The harness is sparse
(one Boolean per trajectory), brittle (collisions, unsupported
families, harness invocation failures), and ungradient. The
distill is dense, robust to harness gaps, and gradient-friendly.
Each compensates for the other's weakness. Neither alone is enough.

## 8 What it taught us

Three transferable lessons.

**Pricing thresholds re-architect choice spaces.** The decision to
distill via nano-medium API rather than self-host a 7B verifier is
load-bearing on a single number — the API per-row cost. That number
moved past the self-hosted floor in May 2026 and stayed there.
Designs that depend on a single pricing threshold should bake in a
revert plan if the threshold inverts. Ours: if nano-medium pricing
reverts to &#36;0.30/M+, retrain on the cached 16-dim labels (no new
API spend), pivot the labeling pass to a self-hosted 7B at the next
corpus refresh.

**Latency at the per-MCTS-expansion granularity is decisive.** The
WM judge head at 23ms is not just a cost optimization — it is a
**different operational regime** than 120ms API latency. The
training-time saving is 5-6 days; the inference-time saving frees
~18% of p95 budget. Anywhere a head is queried per MCTS expansion,
the head must be local. Anywhere a head is queried once per
trajectory (terminal reward), the API is fine.

**Smooth surrogate + signed truth is a robust pattern.** The
harness verdict + 16-dim distill split lets us train against a
gradient-friendly target while anchoring on a ground-truth Boolean.
The pattern generalizes: anywhere a signed-truth signal is expensive
and ungradient, build a cheap dense surrogate and weight them with
a tunable $\beta$. The corpus filter applies the signed truth; the
training loop applies the surrogate; the model learns the
surrogate's shape constrained by the truth's anchor.

The honest postscript: as of 2026-05-19 the realized v4 production
ckpt does not yet train against the 16-dim distill. The plan is
documented, the cost is verified, the integration shape is clear;
the wiring step is open. Same shape as several entries in
HISTORY/34: the design is correct, the artifact is not yet
deployed. We flag it loudly rather than carry the &#36;39.59 number
as a closed line item.
