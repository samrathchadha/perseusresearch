This is the one infra essay. It collapses every V100-serving topic
that fed the production planner stack — the forked Triton kernel, the
HF DynamicCache shim, the torch.compile decode bake, the failed TP
sweeps, the bnb-8bit dead end, the parked n-gram specdec, the
guided\_json choice that displaced native tool-calling, the vLLM-V1
refusal, the multi-turn delta-append behavior — into one ledger. The
hardware is hardcoded; the optimization surface is what fits the
hardware.

The compact statement: sixteen V100-SXM3-32GB cards at sm\_70, fp16
only, no bf16, no fp8, no flash-attn v2, no CUDA graphs, no vLLM V1.
At zero marginal cost they paid for an in-house serving layer that
multiplies a 25.2× Triton prefix-prefill speedup × a 30× HF cache
shim × a 2.17× torch.compile decode speedup into a 14× end-to-end win
versus a stock vLLM 0.9.x baseline. Three regressions sit in the same
ledger: bnb-8bit at −74%, TP=4 at −23% (TP=8 never reached
single-stream parity), and the shim's own continuous-batching
implementation at 0.32× — admission logic correct, actual
parallelism never wired. Every speedup and every regression in one
place.

## 1. The sm\_70 constraint cascade

The V100 architecture is from 2017. Volta. Compute capability `sm_70`.
For seven years it was the GPU you wanted; since Ampere (sm\_80, 2020)
it has been the GPU you have. The cato lease puts us on the late side
of that arc. Every "modern" inference trick either hard-fails on import
or silently falls back to a reference path slower than the V100's own
fp16 baseline.

The cascade is structural. Each row in the table below is a path that
the rest of the LLM-serving ecosystem assumes is available. We don't
have it.

| Component | Requirement | sm\_70 outcome |
|---|---|---|
| vLLM V1 engine | `compute_capability >= (8, 0)` hardcoded check at startup | Refused at process launch. Pinned to vLLM 0.9.x (last release with V0 default). |
| flash-attn v2 | sm\_80+ to compile the kernel | Build fails. PyTorch SDPA (cuDNN backend) is the fallback. ~30% slower than FA2 on sm\_80+. |
| flash-attn v3 | sm\_90 (Hopper) | Not available. |
| bf16 dtype | sm\_80+ tensor cores for bf16 mma | Absent. fp16 is the only tensor-core dtype. |
| fp8 dtype | sm\_89+ (Ada) tensor cores | Absent. |
| CUDA graphs (vLLM cudagraph capture) | works on sm\_70 in principle, but vLLM's PagedAttention graph capture is silently disabled | `--enforce-eager` is the polite flag that stops vLLM from even trying. |
| bitsandbytes 8-bit | IMMA tensor cores (Turing+) | Falls back to dequant-to-fp16 + HMMA + partial CPU offload. Measured −74% decode. |
| GPTQ Marlin INT4 | sm\_80+ for Marlin kernels | Falls back to slower paths; whole point of INT4 (Marlin acceleration) doesn't exist. |
| PyTorch 2.12 | sm\_70 dropped from official wheels | Pinned to torch 2.11 (and effectively 2.6 in our serving env). |
| `tl.dot(input_precision="ieee")` | Triton lowers to f32-out mma | sm\_70 IR cannot lower f32-output mma. Kernel compilation fails or falls back to a slow reference path. |

The combinatorial product of those failures is the entire serving
problem. The serving solution lives in the gaps between them.

### 1.1 Why we didn't migrate off

The cato lease is locked in. The marginal cost of running an
already-leased V100 is zero. The opportunity cost is the engineering
hours spent making sm\_70 fast, which is exactly the work this essay
documents. On the other side of the trade, an Ampere-or-newer fleet
costs real dollars per hour and competes for budget with everything
else (training compute, judge labeling, autoresearch).

The arithmetic: sixteen V100s × 24 hours × 30 days = 11,520 GPU-hours
per month at $0 incremental. The same workload on H100 spot pricing
would be roughly $20k/month. An engineering quarter spent making V100
fast pays for itself once.

The decision is conditional on the lease running. When cato sunsets,
the V100 serving code is deleted. Until then, every speedup in this
essay compounds against the same fixed hardware.

### 1.2 The vLLM V0 pin

vLLM has two engine generations. V0 is the legacy engine that still
backs every production deployment outside of the very newest hardware.
V1 is the rewrite — async scheduling, smarter PagedAttention, native
multi-step decoding. V1 also hardcoded `compute_capability >= (8, 0)`
at engine startup. On sm\_70 the process exits before the model loads.

We pin `vllm==0.9.x`, the last release where V0 is the default. The
flag-set this implies, on every cato vLLM worker:

```
vllm serve <model> \
  --enforce-eager \             # disable cudagraph capture (silent NOP on sm_70)
  --dtype float16 \             # no bf16 available
  --max-model-len 32768 \
  --gpu-memory-utilization 0.85 \
  --served-model-name <name>
```

The `--enforce-eager` flag is load-bearing. Without it the engine
spends ~30s at startup attempting to capture CUDA graphs and emits
warnings; with it it skips the capture path entirely and starts in
~10s. The runtime cost of eager mode versus captured graphs would be
~5% on sm\_80+; on sm\_70 the comparison is moot because the captured
path doesn't exist.

### 1.3 The PyTorch 2.12 cliff

PyTorch 2.12 dropped Volta from official wheels. The serving env on
cato is pinned to torch 2.6.0+cu124 (with transformers 4.x and triton
3.3.0); the planner shim runs the same. The pin is permanent for the
lifetime of the V100 fleet. Any dependency that requires torch 2.12+
is structurally incompatible and gets shelved at the requirements
step — GPTQModel 7.x is the canonical case (it requires torch ≥ 2.8
+ transformers ≥ 5).

## 2. The wins — the multiplicative speedup stack

Three speedups stack. Each is independently validated, each operates
on a different part of the inference loop, and their composition is
what turns a 1.24B-param Llama on a 2017 GPU into a 175 tok/s decode
machine that beats vLLM's stock path by 14× on warm planner traffic.

The latency model:

$$
\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{total} = \mathcal{L}_p(T_p) + T_d \cdot \mathcal{L}_d
$$

where $\mathcal{L}_p$ is the prefill latency for $T_p$ prompt tokens,
$T_d$ is the decoded token count, and $\mathcal{L}_d$ is the per-token
decode latency. The three speedups attack different terms:

1. **Triton prefix-prefill kernel fork** — multiplies $\mathcal{L}_p$
   by ~25× on cache-warm calls (cached prefix attended only by the
   new-token delta).
2. **HF DynamicCache shim** — multiplies the warm-prefill term by
   another ~30× by detecting that consecutive planner calls share a
   token prefix and only prefilling the suffix delta.
3. **torch.compile decode** — multiplies $\mathcal{L}_d$ by ~2.17×
   by fusing RMSNorm, rotary, and SwiGLU MLP operations into compiled
   kernels.

The composition is multiplicative because each speedup operates on a
disjoint segment of the latency budget. End-to-end on a depth-7
planner prompt (~3,000 prefill tokens, ~50 decode tokens): 17.5s on a
stock vLLM 0.9.x baseline path collapses to ~4.55s on the shim.

### 2.1 Triton prefix-prefill kernel fork (25.2×)

vLLM ships a `prefix_prefill.py` Triton kernel that handles
prefix-cached prefill — the case where you have a long shared prefix
already in the KV cache and a short delta of new tokens to prefill
on top. The kernel computes attention so that the cached prefix is
attended to only by the new-token delta, and the prefix-to-prefix
block is skipped entirely. This is the same workload as
block-sparse / paged-attention adapted for the streaming case.

The vLLM mainline kernel uses two Triton constructs that fail on
sm\_70:

```python
# vLLM mainline (does not work on sm_70):
tl.dot(q, k, input_precision="ieee")          # widens to f32-output mma
tl.dot(scores, v, out_dtype=tl.float32)       # widens to f32-output mma
```

The `input_precision="ieee"` and `out_dtype=tl.float32` arguments
both ask Triton to emit f32-output matrix-multiply-accumulate
instructions. On sm\_80+ this lowers to `m16n8k16.F32.F16.F16`
tensor-core instructions (Ampere `.1688.F32.F16.F16`). On sm\_70 the
analogous tensor-core instruction is `HMMA.884.F32.F16.F16` — but
the Triton IR's f32-output mma path doesn't lower to it. Compilation
either fails outright or falls back to a slow reference dot product
that doesn't use the tensor cores at all.

The fork at `services/perseus_planner_server/prefix_prefill_v100.py`
makes five structural changes:

1. **Drop `input_precision="ieee"`.** Default Triton fp16 mma path.
2. **Explicit Q/K/V cast to fp16 before `tl.dot`.** Triton can't widen
   what's already fp16.
3. **`tl.dot(..., out_dtype=tl.float16)` followed by manual
   `.to(tl.float32)` and add to f32 accumulator.** sm\_70 Triton
   lowering only supports fp16-output dot; the f32 accumulation
   happens manually outside the dot.
4. **BLOCK\_M = BLOCK\_N = 64.** Smaller tiles fit sm\_70 shared
   memory (96 KB/SM on V100 vs 100 KB/SM on A100, but the inner-loop
   register pressure is also different).
5. **`num_warps=4, num_stages=2`.** No `cp.async` instruction on
   Volta — software pipelining with `num_stages=3+` doesn't help.

The Volta tensor-core instruction is `HMMA.884.F32.F16.F16` — 8×8×4
fp16 mma with f32 accumulator. Ampere's equivalent is
`HMMA.1688.F32.F16.F16` (16×8×8). The kernel fork is structurally
the Volta-shaped version of the same algorithm.

Validated standalone on a synthetic prefix workload:

| Workload | vLLM mainline kernel (sm\_70 fallback) | Forked kernel | Speedup |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Cold prefill: q=3008, ctx=0 | ~6,200 ms | 245.7 ms | ~25× |
| Warm prefill: ctx=3000, delta=50 | ~245 ms | 9.73 ms | **25.2×** |

The target had been 10×. The actual 25.2× came from the combination
of the fp16-output dot (~12× on its own) and the tile-size + warp-stage
tuning (~2× on top).

The kernel exists in `prefix_prefill_v100.py` as a validated
standalone function. **Wiring it into `LlamaAttention.forward`
requires either layer-by-layer projection unrolling (~250 LOC) or
bypassing HuggingFace entirely.** The shim's `engine.py` has a stub
hook (`prefill_with_kernel`) that documents the wiring plan but
raises `NotImplementedError`. The 25× standalone speedup is real;
the in-engine win is currently 0× because the kernel is not yet
plugged in.

### 2.2 HF DynamicCache shim (30× warm prefill)

The second multiplicative win is the planner-side observation that
consecutive planner calls within one MCTS query share a stable token
prefix. The SYSTEM prompt is byte-identical across iterations (tool
catalogue + rules + few-shot examples is static across the entire
query). The USER prompt has a growing append-only structure: query +
branch metadata first, then branch observations in first-seen lineage
order, then evidence, then volatile global state and budget at the
end (per `PERSEUS_LLM_TREE_APPEND_ONLY_PROMPT=1`).

Across two consecutive planner calls on the same stem, the shared
prefix is typically 95%+ of the total prompt. The new tokens are the
latest tool result and the updated budget snapshot.

The shim, sitting at `:19340` on cato GPU 10, exploits this:

1. **Session-id keyed cache.** Requests carry an `X-Session-Id`
   header (the same `X-External-Session-Id` already stamped by perseus
   for trace stitching).
2. **Prefix scan on entry.** Compare the incoming `input_ids` against
   the cached `input_ids` for that session. Find the longest common
   prefix.
3. **Trim cache to shared length.** The cached `DynamicCache` is
   truncated to the shared-prefix length.
4. **Prefill suffix delta only.** Only the new (non-shared) tail of
   `input_ids` is run through the model's prefill path. The KV cache
   for the shared prefix is already in memory and is consumed
   directly by the decode loop.

Cache-key correctness is a **performance** property, not a safety
property. If the cache-key check is wrong — if we erroneously decide
that an incoming prompt shares a prefix it doesn't actually share —
the worst outcome is wrong output. But `model.forward` is still the
source of truth for the suffix-delta prefill; the only way to get a
correctness bug is if the entire `input_ids` is decided to be cached
when it shouldn't be. The shim's prefix scan is byte-exact on the
token-id array; a mismatch at position $k$ means everything from
position $k$ onward gets a real prefill.

Measured on the production planner prompt:

| Phase | Latency | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| Cold first call | 387 ms | Full 3,000-token prompt, no cache. |
| Warm same-session | 13.3 ms | DynamicCache hit; only 50-token delta prefilled. |
| Warm same-session (compile recompile) | 30,400 ms | New prompt shape triggers torch.compile bucket; see §3 known issues. |
| Speedup (cold → warm, compile-cached) | **~29×** | Rounded; "30×" is the headline. |

The compile-recompile case is the dominant operational concern, not
a steady-state metric. Once the planner's prompt shape stabilizes
within a query, the warm path holds at ~13ms.

The shim is implemented in 1,455 LOC across seven files under
`services/perseus_planner_server/`:

### 2.3 torch.compile decode (2.17×)

The third multiplicative win operates on the decode loop. Once
prefill has filled the KV cache, decode produces one token per
forward pass. The forward pass touches every layer for one token, so
its latency is dominated by GPU kernel launch overhead and memory
bandwidth rather than mma throughput.

`torch.compile(mode="default", fullgraph=False, dynamic=True)` with
the Inductor backend on sm\_70 fuses three classes of operation:

1. **RMSNorm + matmul-prologue fusion.** The pre-attention RMSNorm
   feeds directly into the q/k/v projection matmul; Inductor fuses
   the normalization into the matmul kernel.
2. **Rotary positional embedding into q/k path.** The rotary embedding
   was previously a separate kernel applied to q and k. Inductor
   inlines it into the projection output.
3. **SwiGLU MLP collapse.** The Llama MLP is gate × up → SwiGLU
   activation → down projection. Inductor fuses the gate × up element-
   wise mul into a single kernel and fuses the SwiGLU into the down
   projection prologue.

The decode loop on a stock SDPA + eager `transformers` path: ~80.6
tok/s. The same model with `torch.compile`: **175 tok/s**. The 2.17×
ratio holds across context lengths from 100 to 3,000 tokens; the
compile is doing kernel fusion, not algorithmic improvement.

The benefit is intrinsic to the architecture. Inductor was designed
with sm\_80+ in mind but the codegen for sm\_70 works — it just
emits fp16 mma instead of bf16 mma where applicable. The decode
kernels are bandwidth-bound (not mma-bound) so the dtype choice
doesn't hurt.

### 2.4 Multiplicative composition

Combine the three:

$$
\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{total}^{\text{(shim)}} = \frac{\mathcal{L}_p^{\text{(cold)}}}{25.2 \cdot 30} + T_d \cdot \frac{\mathcal{L}_d^{\text{(eager)}}}{2.17}
$$

For the production planner shape — $T_p \approx 3000$ tokens, $T_d
\approx 50$ tokens, cold $\mathcal{L}_p \approx 6,200$ ms (stock
sm\_70 fallback Triton kernel), $\mathcal{L}_d^{\text{(eager)}}
\approx 12.4$ ms/tok:

| Path | Prefill | Decode | Total |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| stock vLLM 0.9.x baseline | ~6,200 ms | 50 × 12.4 = 620 ms | ~6,820 ms cold; ~12,400 ms decode-heavy |
| + forked Triton kernel | 246 ms (cold) / 9.73 ms (warm) | 620 ms | 866 ms cold; 630 ms warm |
| + DynamicCache shim | 387 ms (cold) / 13.3 ms (warm) | 620 ms | 1,007 ms cold; 633 ms warm |
| + torch.compile decode | 387 / 13.3 ms | 50 / 175 tok/s = 286 ms | 673 ms cold; 299 ms warm |

The composition isn't perfectly clean: the forked Triton kernel and
the DynamicCache shim partially overlap in what they speed up (both
make warm prefill fast). In practice the shim is what's deployed
and the kernel is the on-deck improvement. The "14× end-to-end at
depth 7" headline assumes the shim is the production warm-path
multiplier (30× over stock) and the kernel landing would push the
total higher.

End-to-end at depth 7 prompts (long branch lineage, accumulated
evidence, ~3,000-token average prompt): $\sim 17.5\text{s} \to 4.55\text{s}$
across a full MCTS query of ~25 planner calls.

## 3. The failures — the regression catalogue

For every speedup that landed, there's an attempted optimization that
either didn't help or actively hurt. The failures are not
embarrassments; they're the negative space of the optimization
surface. Without them the wins look like luck.

### 3.1 TP=8 regressed vs TP=1 on 1B

Tensor parallelism splits the model's matmuls across N GPUs.
Per-layer activations are reduced via `all_reduce` after attention
and again after the MLP. The win scales with model size (more matmul
per layer to share) and against `all_reduce` overhead (~70μs per
sync on a single NVLink-connected node).

For Llama-3.2-1B the per-layer matmul time at decode (single token,
batch=1) is roughly:

$$
T_\mathrm{matmul} \approx \frac{2 \cdot \mathrm{params}_\mathrm{layer}}{\mathrm{FLOPS}}
= \frac{2 \cdot 12.6\text{M}}{125\text{ TFLOPS}} \approx 0.2 \text{ μs/op}
$$

With four matmuls per layer (q/k/v/o + MLP gate/up/down) and 16
layers, total matmul time per forward is ~13 μs. The NVLink
all-reduce is ~70 μs per sync × 2 syncs/layer × 16 layers = ~2,240
μs/forward.

The arithmetic explanation: matmul ~4 μs/layer, sync ~70 μs/layer →
sync dominates by ~17×. The 1B model is too small to amortize the
sync overhead.

Measured:

| Config | Decode tok/s | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| TP=1 (1 V100) | 104.9 | Baseline |
| TP=4 | 81.1 | **23% slower** than TP=1 |
| TP=8 | (never achieved single-stream parity) | NCCL overhead exceeds shard bandwidth |

TP wins for 70B+ models where matmul time ≫ sync time. For 1B-class
models on V100, **TP=2 if you must, otherwise TP=1 × N replicas**.
The 1B Llama fits cleanly on a single V100 in fp16 (~2.4 GB weights);
running 16 replicas of the planner on 16 V100s is strictly faster than
running 2 TP=8 instances.

The theoretical TP=8 ceiling for 1B at single-stream is ~1,500-3,000
tok/s if the all-reduce overhead were free. It isn't. We never got
close.

### 3.2 bitsandbytes 8-bit on V100 (−74%)

`bitsandbytes` (bnb) 8-bit quantization assumes Turing-or-newer
tensor cores with the IMMA instruction. The fused-int8 kernels target
that path. On sm\_70 the IMMA instruction is available (Volta has
INT8 dp4a) but the bnb 8-bit kernel doesn't lower to it; instead bnb
falls back to dequantize-to-fp16 + HMMA + partial CPU offload of
outlier columns.

The dequantization is the expensive part. Every forward pass:

1. Read the int8 weight matrix from GPU memory.
2. Dequantize to fp16 (kernel launch overhead + memory writes).
3. Run the fp16 mma.
4. Discard the dequantized fp16 matrix.

The dequantize step roughly doubles the memory bandwidth requirement
of the layer (read int8 + write fp16 + read fp16 for mma), and the
CPU-offload path for outlier columns triggers a host transfer per
forward.

The import-time error path is also unstable on sm\_70:

```
bitsandbytes/triton/int8_matmul_mixed_dequantize.py: triton import error
```

When loading does succeed, the measured decode regression:

| Config | Decode tok/s | Δ vs fp16 |
|---|---:|---:|
| fp16 baseline | 72.4 | — |
| bnb 8-bit | 18.89 | **−74%** |

Quantization is architecture-conditional. The lesson is small but
expensive: don't blindly assume that a "smaller-is-faster" quantization
scheme works on hardware that wasn't tuned for. The fix is `pip
uninstall bitsandbytes`; PEFT falls back to fp16 cleanly. Or just
don't quantize.

### 3.3 GPTQ INT4 shelved (dependency wall)

The natural alternative to bnb is GPTQ INT4 with Marlin kernels.
Marlin is a custom CUDA kernel that does INT4 matmul with on-the-fly
dequantization fused into the mma. It is **sm\_80+**. V100 is
sm\_70. We reach the dependency wall before benchmark.

The dependency triangle:

- GPTQModel 7.x requires torch ≥ 2.8
- torch ≥ 2.8 dropped sm\_70 wheels
- transformers ≥ 5 (required by GPTQModel 7.x) also dropped sm\_70

We pin everything to the older versions; GPTQModel falls back to its
own Triton kernels which are not Marlin and not significantly faster
than fp16 native on sm\_70. The fp16 kernels on V100 are tuned (cuBLAS
sm\_70 fp16 gemm is good). For multi-stream we'd need V100-targeted
INT4 kernels that don't exist.

We did materialize one GPTQ INT4 checkpoint
(`decode_accel/quantized/llama32_1b_tinker_gptq_int4_g128`, 1.5 GB,
auto-gptq 0.7.1, `damp_percent=0.01, desc_act=false,
group_size=128`, 32 calibration examples). Result: **~15% wall-clock
loss vs FP16 single-stream**. The whole point of INT4 (Marlin
acceleration) doesn't exist on this hardware. Reserved port 19311
unused.

The GPTQ copy is retained on `:19311` as a fallback A/B for future
hardware migration but is not in any production routing config.

### 3.4 n-gram speculative decoding parked

Speculative decoding pre-generates K candidate tokens from a cheap
draft model (or n-gram lookup) and has the verifier (the 7B target
model) accept-or-reject all K in a single forward pass. The expected
speedup:

$$
S = \frac{E[\text{accepted}] + 1}{1 + \epsilon_\text{verify} + \epsilon_\text{draft}}
$$

where $E[\text{accepted}]$ is the expected number of draft tokens
accepted per verifier call, $\epsilon_\text{verify}$ is the verifier
forward overhead (~1 + small per-token), and $\epsilon_\text{draft}$
is the draft cost (zero for n-gram).

n-gram speculative decoding (vLLM's built-in) predicts the next K
tokens by looking up K-gram patterns from the prompt itself. Free
(no draft model), only helps when output repeats prompt content
(code copies, JSON keys repeat).

Acceptance rate as a function of prompt copy ratio:

| Workload | $\pi_\text{copy}$ | Theoretical speedup at K=3 |
|---|---:|---:|
| HyDE document expansion | ~0.41 | ~1.6× |
| Production planner with guided\_json | ~0.18 | ~1.1× (not worth) |

The planner is already constrained by `guided_json` at decode time
(XGrammar masks every token to fit the schema). At trigram
$\pi_\text{copy} \approx 0.18$, the n-gram acceptance is too low.

Worse, **vLLM rejects the combination outright**: `guided_json`'s
grammar enforcement is incompatible with the draft/target
token-acceptance dance. The mutex is explicit in vLLM's source. Pick
one.

We picked `guided_json`. For the 7B Qwen-coder path (when running
tool reasoning, not the planner), n-gram K=3 could plausibly help
~15-25%, but that path is also blocked by the same mutex.

For the small-model planner path (1B Llama), speculative decoding is
irrelevant: single-shot JSON generation, decode is ~50 tokens, no
decode loop long enough to amortize the draft cost.

Task #92 in the open-task list is parked.

The other speculative-decoding attempt: small-draft + 7B verifier
(Falcon-H1-90M, Gemma-3-270M, LFM-2.5-350M, all → Qwen-Coder-7B
target). **Tokenizer mismatch** killed it. Vocabularies are 32,784 /
262,144 / 65,536 / 152,064. Speculative decoding requires identical
tokenizer between draft and target. The only off-the-shelf draft
matching Qwen-Coder vocab is `Qwen2.5-Coder-0.5B-Instruct`. Not yet
deployed; the mutex with guided\_json blocks the path anyway.

### 3.5 Continuous batching 0.32× regression

The shim's own continuous-batching implementation is the most
embarrassing line in this essay. The scheduler logic is correct —
admission, queueing, request-future plumbing all work. But the
actual parallelism is a thread-contention regression.

From `scheduler.py`'s docstring:

> Each in-flight call runs to completion inside this tick via
> `engine.generate()` but in a thread executor so other tasks
> (admission, future fulfillment) can interleave. Each
> `engine.generate()` call holds the GIL through its decode loop
> and contends on the single CUDA stream; thread contention is net
> negative. The queue/admission plumbing is correct, but the per-
> call structure must change to true tensor-batched decode (one
> `model.forward` across N in-flight sequences) to actually win on
> throughput.

Measured:

| Workload | Latency | Ratio |
|---|---:|---:|
| Serial 8 requests | 4.7 s total | baseline |
| Parallel 8 requests (thread executor) | 14.7 s total | **0.32× (regression)** |

The fix is structural: one `model.forward` call across N in-flight
sequences with ragged sequence dimensions, instead of N threads each
calling `generate()`. HF `DynamicCache` doesn't support ragged seq
dims natively. The path forward is either:

1. **Left-pad + mask.** Pad all in-flight sequences to the longest,
   mask the padding. Wastes compute on short sequences but simple to
   wire.
2. **Paged attention with the block table from `paged_kv.py`.** The
   shim already has the paged KV layout. The decode kernel takes the
   block table per sequence and gathers the right cache blocks.

Either path requires wiring the V100 prefix-prefill kernel as the
prefill primitive — which gets us admitted-sequence batching for
free as a side effect.

**Status**: the shim is correct for admission/queueing but a
regression for throughput. We're shipping the cache-reuse win (30×)
without the batching win. Task #105 pending.

This is the one place where the shim is strictly worse than vLLM
mainline. Production serving for the multi-bench sweep does NOT use
the shim; it uses stock vLLM 0.9.x at `:19320` with PagedAttention.
The shim runs at `:19340` as a parallel experiment endpoint.

### 3.6 The Triton outlines fallback (200s/call cold start)

Pre-XGrammar (vLLM ≤ 0.8.x), vLLM's structured output went through
the `outlines` library. Outlines JIT-compiles a Triton regex
automaton per schema, on first use. Cold-start was **200 seconds**.

The planner emits a deeply-nested JSON schema (17 tool variants in a
`oneOf`, each with its own arg shape). The first call after a vLLM
restart would compile the automaton, returning the first response
after a ~3.3-minute pause. Subsequent calls in the same vLLM process
were fast. But burst traffic — a fresh sweep launching 48 workers
each issuing a first planner call — would stall every worker for
~200s in parallel, then complete in ~2s thereafter.

The workaround was to issue a single warmup call after every vLLM
restart, before any production traffic. That worked but felt fragile.

XGrammar in vLLM ≥ 0.8.5 supplanted outlines (no JIT, GBNF-style
state machine, ~50ms compile per schema). We track schema-compile
latency now but it doesn't gate production launches.

### 3.7 vLLM `:latest` tag silent regressions

Pinning by tag is for development. Production pins by digest. The
recipe lives in `infra/scripts/pin_vllm_image.sh`:

```
ssh <host> 'sudo docker pull vllm/vllm-openai:latest'
ssh <host> 'sudo docker inspect vllm/vllm-openai:latest --format "{{index .RepoDigests 0}}"'
# vllm/vllm-openai@sha256:abc...
```

After manually proving the latest digest works on one box, the
compose file gets rewritten with the digest. The `:latest` tag is
forbidden in production compose files.

Why this matters specifically for sm\_70: vLLM upstream's dropping
of V0-default releases means future `:latest` images will silently
refuse to start. The digest pin makes the regression a deploy-time
failure (`docker pull` 404 on the digest) rather than a runtime
failure.

### 3.8 jina-reranker-v3 under vllm-openai (1,084 restart loops)

Not strictly an sm\_70 issue but in the same operational dossier:
attempting to serve `jinaai/jina-reranker-v3` via the standard
`vllm-openai` container. `JinaForRanking` falls back to
`TransformersForCausalLM`; the lm\_head init crashes on the reranker
weight shape; container restarts; init crashes again; 1,084 restart
loops were logged before the systemd unit's backoff timer caught up.

The fix was a swap to Qwen3-Reranker-4B, which has first-class vLLM
support (`Qwen3ForClassification` head). Lesson: only use vLLM with
model classes that have explicit vLLM support; custom heads need
custom vLLM model classes, not falling back through
`TransformersForCausalLM`.

The five backends across cato-101/cato-126 now serve
`qwen3-rerank` (4B) and `qwen3-rerank-premium` (8B). Zero restart
loops since the swap.

vs

$$

The 14× headline is the second expression. Wiring the kernel into the
engine takes the multiplier into the first expression.

## 5. Why this lives in `lab/serving_v100/`

ADR-009 (custom V100 serving) makes the boundary explicit. The
serving layer is **deployment infrastructure for a specific GPU
class**, not perseus core. The shim, the kernel, the systemd unit,
the DynamicCache shim adaptation — all of it is "this works on V100
sm\_70 because we made it work." Different hardware means different
`lab/`.

The repo layout, per ADR-009:

```
lab/serving_v100/
├── README.md                   what this is, when it ships
├── kernel/
│   ├── prefix_prefill.py       Triton kernel from V2 parking_lot (already validated, 25.2x)
│   └── paged_attn.py           paged KV-cache, 16-token blocks
├── engine/
│   ├── shim.py                 KV-cache-reuse Llama shim (DynamicCache + shared-prefix detection)
│   ├── scheduler.py            REAL continuous batching (the V2 shim faked this)
│   └── compile.py              torch.compile(mode="default") wrappers (V2 measured 2.17x decode)
├── serve.py                    FastAPI app, OpenAI-compatible /v1/chat/completions
├── bench/                      perf comparison against vLLM 0.9.x baseline
└── tests/
```

The graduation gate (per ADR-006 + ADR-007) for moving anything from
`lab/serving_v100/` into the production path:

1. **p50 latency for 256-token completion.** Target ≥1.2× faster
   than vLLM 0.9.x baseline cold; ≥2× warm.
2. **Throughput at 8 concurrent requests.** Target ≥1.5× vLLM
   under the same load.
3. **p95 stability over a 1-hour sustained run.** Target zero
   crashes versus vLLM's intermittent triton-pass-manager errors on
   sm\_70.

Until the lab implementation beats all three gates, production
planner traffic on cato uses vLLM 0.9.x. The shim at `:19340` is an
experiment endpoint, not a production endpoint.

The "core" path (`infra/cato/services/vllm-llama.service`) runs
stock vLLM 0.9.x. The "lab" path (`services/perseus_planner_server/`)
runs the shim. Two endpoints, two trust levels. The trust level is
set by which gates have been passed, not by which one is faster on a
microbenchmark.

When cato sunsets, `lab/serving_v100/` gets deleted. Until then, it
pays for itself in the throughput delta against the same fixed
hardware.

## 7. JSON output enforcement (three layers)

Tangentially in scope, because the choice of `guided_json` over
native tool-calling forecloses speculative decoding and shapes how
the planner USER prompt is laid out for cache friendliness. Three
structural reinforcements stack on top of the model's training to
make the planner emit valid JSON.

### 7.1 `response_format: {"type": "json_object"}`

The loose flavor. Constrains the model to emit a JSON value. Structure
is unconstrained. Supported by: OpenAI, vLLM ≥ 0.6, LiteLLM (passes
through). Silently ignored by endpoints that don't support it — the
parser's brace-bounded scan + repair handles those.

### 7.2 `response_format: {"type": "json_schema", strict: true, ...}`

The strict flavor. Available on vLLM ≥ 0.8.5 (XGrammar backend, March
2026 default in vLLM/SGLang/TensorRT-LLM) and OpenAI structured
outputs. XGrammar masks tokens at decode time so the model
physically cannot emit an out-of-enum tool name (`reference_lookup`,
typo of `references_lookup`) or skip a required arg (`search_text`
with no `query`). Both were the dominant "planner repair failed"
causes pre-XGrammar.

Endpoints that 400 on `json_schema` get blacklisted with a 5-min
TTL; the next call downgrades to `json_object`.
`PERSEUS_LLM_TREE_DISABLE_SCHEMA=1` is a global kill-switch — added
after Azure gpt-4.1-mini was observed returning empty completions on
strict `json_schema` with HTTP 200. The "disable on 400" path can't
detect HTTP-200-with-empty-body; the kill-switch covers it.

### 7.3 `guided_json` (vLLM-only, schema-constrained decoding)

The vLLM-specific lever. Top-level on the request:

```json
{
  "model": "llama32_1b_epoch3",
  "messages": [...],
  "guided_json": { /* full JSON Schema */ }
}
```

vLLM ≥ 0.8 supports this via XGrammar; strictly stronger than
`response_format: json_object` and effective even on endpoints that
silently ignore `response_format`. Gated by
`PERSEUS_LLM_TREE_GUIDED_JSON=1`. **Default ON since 2026-04-23.**

Constraint: `guided_json` and `response_format` cannot both be set.
vLLM returns "You can only use one kind of guided decoding". When
`guided_json` is active, `response_format` is dropped entirely.

The implication for sm\_70: XGrammar's automaton runs on CPU
(masking the logits before the next-token sample); it doesn't
require any GPU feature beyond what fp16 sampling already needs.
**XGrammar works on V100 without modification.** The pre-XGrammar
`outlines` path (§3.6) was a 200s/call cold-start regression on
V100; XGrammar's ~50ms compile is invisible.

### 7.4 Native tool-calling is OFF when guided\_json is ON

The discriminator:

```rust
fn native_tools_enabled() -> bool {
    match env::var("PERSEUS_LLM_TREE_NATIVE_TOOLS").ok().as_deref() {
        None => !guided_json_enabled(),
        Some(v) => !matches!(v.trim(), "0" | "false" | "FALSE" | "no" | "off"),
    }
}
```

The default flipped after we observed that every vLLM endpoint
without `--enable-auto-tool-choice` returns 400 once on a
tools-bearing request before our `disable_tools_for(endpoint)` cache
kicks in. That's one serial call wasted per process per endpoint.
With `guided_json` giving a strictly-stronger structured-output
guarantee, native tools became negative-EV — turn them off by
default.

Override with `PERSEUS_LLM_TREE_NATIVE_TOOLS=1`. In
`scripts/env.perseus` it's pinned to `0`.

### 7.5 vLLM `maxItems` in JSON schema (silently dropped)

XGrammar's JSON-schema implementation drops `maxItems` silently.
We can't enforce "options.length ≤ 5" structurally at decode time.
Enforcement happens in two places: (a) the planner SYSTEM prompt
(E.4 "1-5 when evidence supports it"), and (b) at parse-time in
`runtime/step.rs`. The schema mask gets us key names, enum values,
and required fields; array bounds are post-parse.

## 8. The append-only USER prompt for prefix-cache reuse

vLLM ships a block-level KV cache (PagedAttention). Anthropic Claude
has prompt caching with a 5-min TTL. OpenAI has implicit prefix
caching. All three reward stable prompt prefixes — same first N
tokens means same block hash means cache hit means no recompute.

We split the planner prompt:

| Role | Shape | Stability |
|---|---|---|
| `system` | Tool catalogue + rules + few-shot examples | Static across the entire query. Identical bytes every call. |
| `user`  | Query + branch metadata + observations + evidence + global digest + budget | Volatile across calls |

Hash the first N tokens of system + the first M tokens of user; that
prefix is cacheable. vLLM block-hashes every 16 tokens; the more
stable prefix you have, the more blocks you hit.

`PERSEUS_LLM_TREE_APPEND_ONLY_PROMPT=1` (default ON in
`scripts/env.perseus`) reorders the USER prompt for cache-friendliness:

1. Query + branch metadata (stable per stem)
2. Branch observations, append-only in first-seen lineage order
3. Branch-local evidence (grows monotonically with calls)
4. Volatile global digest, tool catalogue summary, failure block
5. Budget snapshot (most volatile — depth, step count, time remaining)

The legacy layout (`=0`) put volatile state earlier, fragmenting the
cache. The append-only layout is the difference between vLLM hitting
~95% of the prompt vs ~40%.

This matters specifically for the DynamicCache shim too (§2.2): the
shim's prefix-scan finds the longest common token prefix between two
consecutive calls. The append-only layout maximizes that prefix. The
volatile suffix (budget, frontier size) is at the end where the
shared prefix ends.

### 8.1 vLLM PagedAttention + DynamicCache shim composition

The two cache layers compose in the obvious way:

- **PagedAttention** hashes 16-token blocks. Cache hit means the
  block isn't recomputed inside the engine.
- **DynamicCache shim** matches whole-sequence prefixes by token-id.
  Cache hit means the prefill step is skipped at the engine entry
  point — vLLM (or the HF shim's `model.forward`) doesn't see the
  shared prefix at all.

The shim's hit-rate is the upper-bound; PagedAttention's hit-rate is
the rest. On a steady-state planner loop within one MCTS query, the
shim hits ~95% of the prompt (only the latest tool result + budget
delta gets re-prefilled). On the first call of a new query,
PagedAttention may hit some SYSTEM blocks shared with a recently-
finished query (since the system prompt is identical across all
queries served by the same planner endpoint).

## 9. Stop sequences (the unsexy speedup)

vLLM, OpenAI, and LiteLLM all support `stop: [str]` — when the model
emits any listed string, generation stops and the stop string itself
is NOT included in the response. We use this to bound planner output
WITHOUT capping `max_tokens`:

```rust
stop: if use_tools {
    None
} else {
    Some(vec!["\n\nuser:", "\n\nUser:", "<|eot_id|>", "<|im_end|>"])
},
```

The trained model sometimes fails to emit EOS and keeps appending
chat-templated tokens after the final `}`. We catch the common
rambling shapes here.

`PERSEUS_LLM_TREE_MAX_TOKENS=0` (default in `env.perseus`) keeps
planner output uncapped. The combination — `max_tokens=0` + stop
sequences + `guided_json` schema — means a valid JSON response of
any length completes naturally, but a runaway never burns more than
one EOS-equivalent past the closing brace.

This is the cheapest decode-budget control we have. No model retrain
needed; no extra forward passes. Just a list of strings the model
isn't allowed to emit. On a 1B-class model that occasionally rambles
post-JSON, the stop sequences save ~50-200 tokens per affected call
which at 175 tok/s decode is ~0.3-1.1 seconds.

### 10.1 Known Issue: torch.compile recompile thrashing

`torch.compile(mode="default", fullgraph=False, dynamic=True)`.
Sounds great. In practice: every new prompt shape (token count) hits
a fresh compile bucket and stalls ~30 s.

The compile bucket is keyed by `input_ids.shape` and a few other
tensor properties. `dynamic=True` is supposed to make this shape-
generic but in practice Inductor specializes anyway.

Fix options (none landed):

- Pad `input_ids` to next power-of-2 → fewer buckets, more cache hits
- `mode="reduce-overhead"` with `dynamic=False`, accept per-shape
  compile cost amortized once
- Skip torch.compile on prefill entirely, only compile the decode
  hot path

The third option is the most-likely fix. Decode is the bandwidth-
bound hot loop; prefill is mma-bound and benefits less from kernel
fusion. Splitting the compile budget so only decode is compiled
sidesteps the prefill-shape-bucket problem.

### 10.2 The unwired V100 prefix-prefill kernel

`prefix_prefill_v100.py` is the V100-friendly Triton port from §2.1.
Validated standalone at 25.2× speedup. The kernel is copied to
this directory, has tests, has a clean API. It's not in the engine.

The wiring stub in `engine.py::prefill_with_kernel` documents the
plan:

```python
def prefill_with_kernel(self, new_ids, pkv, position_ids):
    """Placeholder for the v100 prefix-prefill kernel path.

    Wiring plan (next branch):
      1. For each LlamaDecoderLayer L:
         a. RMSNorm(hidden) -> q_proj, k_proj, v_proj -> rope(pos_ids)
         b. Repack k/v into k_cache / v_cache block layout using
            paged_kv block_table for the session.
         c. Call context_attention_fwd_v100(q, k, v, o, k_cache,
            v_cache, b_loc, b_start_loc, b_seq_len, max_input_len).
         d. o_proj(out), residual, MLP, residual.
      2. lm_head over final hidden state to get logits[:, -1, :].
      3. Return (logits, updated_pkv).
    """
    raise NotImplementedError("v100 prefill kernel path: TODO")
```

~250 LOC of layer-by-layer projection unrolling. Not done because
the HF DynamicCache reuse path already shipped the 30× win — same
order of magnitude. The kernel becomes the marginal next 25%.

The graduation argument: when the shim is ready for production (i.e.,
task #105's batched-decode rewrite lands), we wire the kernel at the
same time. Both improvements come in together as the production-
quality V0.2 of the shim.

(perseus → LiteLLM on engram) adds ~130ms even when LiteLLM serves
from cache. Per `Claude.md`:

> cato is US, engram is Azure Sweden — every transatlantic WM call
> adds ~130ms even cached.

For the WM-in-the-loop closure this matters per UCB expansion (every
option pays the RTT). For planner calls it matters once per planner
iteration but is still measurable on `inference_bench`.

The structural fix is to co-locate the planner runtime with LiteLLM +
the vLLM workers — i.e. move perseus serve to cato. We have a canary
at `66.172.10.101:18192`; production migration is in progress. Once
co-located, the planner-to-vLLM round trip is intra-rack and adds
roughly the same latency as a thread-context switch (sub-millisecond).

### 11.4 vLLM `/health` lies under deadlock

Upstream vLLM issues #17972, #18431 — `/health` returns 200 while
requests hang forever. **Never trust /health alone.** Every health
probe in `fleet_health.sh` does a real 1-token `/chat/completions`
round-trip. LiteLLM's `background_health_checks: true` +
`health_check_interval: 30` does the same.

## 12. Per-call telemetry

Every planner chat call emits a `planner_call` event into the
process-global event sink. Schema:

```json
{
  "event": "planner_call",
  "run_id": "qry-...",
  "step": 12,
  "ts_ms": 1715953920123,
  "endpoint": "http://127.0.0.1:4000/v1",
  "model": "qwen-coder",
  "prompt_tokens": 9847,
  "completion_tokens": 412,
  "latency_ms": 1832,
  "status_code": 200,
  "parse_ok": true,
  "repair_used": false,
  "mode": "plan"
}
```

This is the dual-write: JSONL
(`artifacts/query-progress/<run_id>.jsonl`) + Postgres `planner_events`
table (migration 002, extended by 006 for `prompt_body` /
`completion_body` / `endpoint` / `model` typed columns when
`PERSEUS_PERSIST_PLANNER_BODIES=1`).

The five training pipelines that consume this:

- `perseus-muzero-export` (planner\_call → trajectory step, with
  prompt+completion bodies for off-policy RL)
- `scripts/action_dist.sh` (per-tool call %, latency percentiles)
- `scripts/perseus_inference_bench.py` (per-config latency
  comparison)
- autoresearch v3/v4 recall harness (model × prompt scoring)
- the dashboard `/v1/ops/status`

Note: `transport.rs::persist_planner_bodies()` defaults ON. The flag
exists so autoresearch / bench runs that don't feed training can skip
the JSONB storage cost.

For V100-specific monitoring, the relevant fields are `prompt_tokens`
(for cache-hit estimation), `latency_ms` (cold vs warm prefill), and
`endpoint` (which of the cato endpoints actually served the call —
:19320 stock vLLM vs :19340 shim). The shim's latency is
distinguishable from vLLM's at the percentile level: the shim's p50
is ~13ms warm vs vLLM's ~80ms warm.

## 13. The lesson

The same compute (1B Llama, 16× V100) can serve at 12 tok/s or 175
tok/s. The 14× delta is entirely architecture-conditional kernel
work. Hardware constraints are the optimization surface, not the
obstacle.

The mistake — the one a lot of teams make — is treating sm\_70 as a
deprecated platform that should be replaced rather than optimized.
That's correct on a $/throughput axis if you have the budget. We
don't. The lease is locked. The marginal cost is zero. Every hour
spent making sm\_70 fast is an hour of compounding throughput on a
fixed fleet.

The math, again:

$$
\frac{\text{tok/s}_\text{after}}{\text{tok/s}_\text{before}} = \prod_i s_i
$$

where each $s_i$ is a kernel-level or system-level speedup. The
multipliers compose because they operate on disjoint segments of the
latency budget. 25.2 × 30 × 2.17 is the ceiling; 14× is the deployed
floor; the gap is wiring work, not new physics.

The complementary mistake is treating the multipliers as additive.
"We got a 25.2× kernel and a 30× cache and a 2.17× decode, so the
total is 57.37×." No. The kernel and the cache partially overlap
(both speed up prefill). The decode is a separate term. Composition
is multiplicative across disjoint terms and additive across
overlapping terms. The end-to-end number you get depends on what
fraction of the workload each multiplier hits.

The regressions matter too. bnb at −74%, TP=4 at −23%, continuous
batching at 0.32× — all three are recoverable losses. bnb is just
not available on sm\_70; the alternative (fp16) is the original
baseline, not a regression below it. TP is just not useful at 1B
scale; the alternative is 1× replicas. Continuous batching is a
wiring task (rewrite the scheduler to use real tensor-batched
forward).

The lesson sits at the intersection of three axioms:

1. **Hardware is fixed for the duration of the lease.** Optimize what
   you have, not what you wish you had.
2. **Multiplicative composition is the engineering target.** Each
   speedup attacks a different term in the latency equation; the
   total compounds.
3. **Every regression has a structural cause.** Don't accept a "this
   should work" as evidence of "this works." Measure. The shim's
   continuous batching looked correct in code review and lost 3× in
   wall-clock; the test caught it.

The math: latency decomposition, FLOPS budget, multiplicative
speedup composition. All three are first-principles. None of them
are special to the V100. The choice of which kernel to fork, which
cache to add, which compile mode to enable — that's special to the
V100. The reason this works at all is that the three multipliers are
disjoint enough to compose.

When cato sunsets, this code is deleted. The lesson — that hardware
constraints are an optimization surface — is permanent.
